(2024a) explains, that the compositional strategy used by Proba reflects
the exegetical method of typology: by reusing particular Vergilian verses
for biblical characters, Proba creates a typological relationship whereby a
Vergilian type both prefigures and is fulfilled by a biblical antitype. Her
article now proposes an extended model of typology, in which the type
not only prefigures the antitype, but also enfigures it, providing the reader
with a conceptual paradigm through which to understand a supernatural
reality. In the baptism scene (380–414), which serves as a case study, Proba
reuses passages that in the Aeneid describe female characters, hinting at
a feminine typological Trinity. Watson (2024b) writes that Proba’s Cento
contains a corruption at line 42, sometimes printed as two half-lines
separated by a lacuna. A novel reconstruction can be based upon the six
days of the biblical creation, summarized in reverse. Watson presents two
possible variants of the reconstruction and evaluates them on textual,
metrical, compositional, and contextual grounds.
