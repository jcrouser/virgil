Coleiro considers the Aeneid as a political publication and allegory borrowing colours
from
a distant
imaginary past;
its meaning bears on
the
present with almost mystical suggestiveness. Turnus' death is Vergil's apology
for Octavian's not having spared Antony; Octavia becomes the historical substitute for Pallas.
Di Cesare and Dimock regard Aeneas' choice of fury at the
close as a character fault; a man of love resorts to the passion for revenge at
the close. Gransden provides valuable guidelines for the allegorizing process
(in contrast with Binder's Aeneas und Augustus) and indicates how we may
discover new relevance and meanings. Highet's study of speech and narrative
is helpful and enlightening. Horsfall cites historical allusions inthe epic which
accent the Rome-Carthage hostility, and pleads for fresh appreciation of factors in the creation of Vergil's Dido. The tradition has nothing to do with
Greek
literary heroines or with Catullus' Ariadne; the roots lie in Roman
history and prejudice. Violence, greed, duplicity and hatred are the hallmarks
of an unromantic historical element, a recollection of the old hatred of the
Punic Wars. Sordi's volume provides a research group study of the integration
of Italy inthe Roman state as reflected in the poets Horace, Propertius, and
Vergil. All are unanimous in identifying the old Roman virtues with those of
Italy; V. associates the more civilised qualities with
Etruria and
Latium.
Williams' entertaining article shows that Henry's monumental commentary,
despite its idiosyncrasies, often bizarre, benefited Vergilian scholarship.
