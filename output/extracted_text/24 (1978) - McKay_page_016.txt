76
VERGILIUS
Cugusi detects evidence of Vergilian quotations from a school manual; the
grammar and prosody are faulty. Currie compares R. S. Thomas' twentiethcentury
poetry
with
Vergil's. Thomas' Wales and Vergil's Campania are
worlds apart but there are common elements: the hortatory tone of an instructor or preacher, and a common religious atmosphere. The strains are
Vergilian
but Thomas has disclaimed Vergilian influence. Edmunds shows
that several of Robert Frost's poems derive from Vergil's Georgias and Eclogues. Garton's study of a 17th century cento on the Resurrection of the
Body shows that the learned cleric drew on Vergil's major opera, and oddly
enough, on Mephaeus Vegius' Supplementum to the Aeneid. Garton provides
a translation along with the Latin mosaic-work of the cento. Jan Kott detects
Vergilian
influence in Shakespeare's Tempest: both poets were concerned
with a tempest-tossed hero, insearch of a New World. Shakespeare was sensitive to the paradigm of the journey by sea and saw Everyman associations in
the journey through penance to salvation and the attainment of the Promised
Land. But Shakespeare not only invoked the Vergilian example, he challenged
and finally rejected it. La Marca's article demonstrates that the funeral games
of lacobo Sannazaro were inspired by Aeneid 5 and Iliad 23. Lee's comparison of Nahum Tate's libretto for Henry Purcell's Dido and Aeneas (1689)
with Vergil's poem sheds light on tastes and standards of both authors and
their ages.
Purcell
wrote
his opera for Josias Priest's Boarding School for
Young Gentlewomen and employed Tate for the libretto. Tate provided a
romanticized
hero and
heroine.
Romano's paper noted that Renaissance
English pastoral was dependent on commentaries provided to illuminate Vergil's frequently baffling text. Two approaches may be detected in the commentaries:
allegorical and rhetorical. Renaissance English commentaries inspired
two versions
of pastoral, those of Spenser, and those of Sidney.
Schleiner shows that Crashaw and Sidney stressed filial piety during the retreat from Troy, whereas late
17th and
18th
century
writers were more
concerned with the domestic pathos attaching to Ascanius and Creusa. Emblem books and engravings support the thesis.

A. G. McKay
