and situation. Stabryla's study, coupled with that of Michael Wigodsky
(158), marks significant progress along a difficult path. Stewart (93) interprets A. 1,461-2, which Klingner called 'dftring, tentative and mysterious'
with the translation: 'and the universe has sympathy for us, and deeds
of the past do affect men's minds'. The events of the past (mortalia)
do not lose their power to affect our minds in the present. Rev. R.A.
Sullivan (94) examines^in. 3, 571ff., 7, 413ff., 783-8, for volcanic associations, a nearby phenomenon for Vergil on Neapolitan Posillipo. Turnus
is characteristically compact of fire but Aeneas shares the same phenomenon at critical junctures. Takho-Godi (95) finds that the chthonic powers,
however discordant, impetuous, irrational, and subversive, play a substantial role in the epic as a stylistic and antagonistic feature. Agathe Thornton
(96) argues that Vergil was deeply interested in moral instruction, particularly of the young, that he was concerned with the structure of moral
values in an earthly and cosmological setting, and that he welcomed the
challenge of Homeric poetry to his creative genius. But the prime motivation to epic composition was educational. Tracy (97) concentrates on
omens and divination in Vergil's opera and argues that they are not to
be viewed as a matter of literary or political etiquette, but as a positive
directional force. Their prevalence in Aen. 1-6 diminishes in Aen. 7-12,
where the colours are brighter and the initiative is human. Aen. 6 may
be viewed as an avowal of Vergil's desertion of Epicurus for modified
Stoicism with its concomitant doctrine of Providence and divine concern
for human affairs. Van Wees (98) examines geographical names as stylistic
ornament, finds several categories of their usage, and extols them as
marks of scholarship and important insights into the poet's desire for
euphony, and into the range of his associational thinking.
