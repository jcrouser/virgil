Arrigoni's study offers a rich and tidy array of literary and archaeological
evidence regarding Camilla. Religion (the Diana Cult at Aricia) and poetry are
richly intermingled in a stimulating study of the Amazon and Priestess nature
of the Volscian commander. Arrigone's volume is required reading. Belfiore
studies seven occasions when Aeneas embraces, or longs to embrace, members
of his family. A comparison of the passages reveals one way in which Vergil
portrays emotional change in Aeneas. Della Corte and Feeney are both concerned with Juno, as personality and divine agent, and with her antipathy to
Aeneas and the Roman cause, and not only in Vergil. Feeney argues that
Juno's unmanageable and disquieting nature resists governance; the forces
represented by the goddess are still in tension at the close of the epic: the
good and evil principles that hold the universe in shifting balance find their
embodiment in Juno's power. Moles uses Aristotle's definition of hamartia as
a blueprint for gauging Dido's moral responsibility for her downfall. Aeneid
4.165-72 signals the culpa (or hamartia) which finally destroys the Carthaginian Queen. Her sexual misdemeanour, her submission to Aeneas out of
wedlock, was determining. Aeneid 4.169-72 and 393 are, in Moles's view,
moral signposts. Muecke defines Vergilian irony and by using Aristotle's analysis of the elements of tragic plot, shows that this dramatic irony is basic to
the structure of Vergil's 'tragedy.' Foreshadowing, in Aeneid 1, creates the
sense of dramatic irony which infuses the tragic story. Németh takes a synoptic view of Catullus' Ariadne, Vergil's Dido, and Ovid's Ariadne. West argues
that Dido's one-time faithful love turned to guilty forgetfulness of Sychaeus
is heightened by parallels and contrast with Andromache's fidelity to the
dead Hector. Dido's infirmity is also the infirmity of Carthage. Dyck's treatment of Sychaeus explores the manifold uses Vergil finds for the deceased
husband: to rouse Dido to leave Carthage without taint of treason; to provide
a hindrance to an involvement with Aeneas; to facilitate Dido's transition
from life to death; and to enhance, in death, her heroic stature.
