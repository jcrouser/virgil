Von Albrecht's study of the epic similes isbrief but perceptive. Chausserie-Laprée's article grapples with the phonic architecture and sonorities of
the epic hexameter. Currie reflects on symmetry as a constant feature of the
phenomenal
world,
particularly in geography,
and
its
effect on Vergil's
creative genius and poetic organization. Delaunois examines extracts from
Aeneid 4, 6, and 9, Bucolics 1, 3, 5, 9, and Georgics 1, 2, and 4. Hartigan 's
study of animal similes remarks that Vergil portrays the Trojans with images
drawn from non-hostile animals, the Latin peoples as helpless creatures; lion
similes are reserved for Aeneas' strongest opponent. Finally, Vergil uses animal similes (bullock, hunting ground) for Aeneas, 'domesticated' creatures for
the 'civilized' hero. Tables detail the use of animal similes in Homer's epics
and the Aeneid. Hough discusses monosyllabic verse endings in the epic, 138
in toto; Vergil employs them, however sparsely, to impart an imagery of
power and the brand of his consummate artistry. Minissale studies ambiguity
inthe Georgics. Raabe studies poetic imagery in the context of death inthe
works of Vergil. Pages 63-141 contain an illuminating, immensely valuable
study of the epic passages.
