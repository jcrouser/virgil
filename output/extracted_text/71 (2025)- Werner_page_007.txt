Berti explains how allusions to the figure of Aratus and to his poem
are useful in defining the genre, poetics, and structure of the Eclogues.
Egan’s reading of Ecl. 3.76–79, informed by two early readers of the
poem (Horace, Serm. 1.44–45; and Ovid, Trist. 2.1.533–38), leads to a
reinterpretation that deviates from conventional modern readings. Merrill
makes a case for reading Ecl. 4 not as a prediction of the advent of a golden
age, but as an entertaining satire on such prognostications, intended to
educate the reader—especially Augustus—as to how illusory they are. The
resemblances between Ecl. 4 and Horace, Epod. 16 (which Merrill also
reads as satirical) arguably bolster this reading. In Ecl. 2, Sherry suggests,
Vergil takes advantage of the lexical range of condo, a verb denoting poetic
composition and preservation, and uses inconditus to mark Corydon as
displaced both physically (country vs. city) and generically (bucolic vs.
elegy).
