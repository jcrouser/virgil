Eleanor Winsor Leach argues that fortunatus (= M. Terentius Varro) and
felix (Geo. 2.490, 493) both denote Vergil's relationship with literary predecessors who were concerned with the voluptas attendant on a perception of
order in the environment. Geymonat detects reverberations of the Calabrian
ranges not only at Georgics 3.219, but elsewhere in Vergil's opera as well;
there are even recollections in the epic. GrassPs article on the Norie plague is
perceptive and helpful. Bettini's account of Aristaeus' epyllion is a competent
review. Brown vindicates the astronomical accuracy of Vergil and emends
Piscis (Geo. 4.234)
to Pestis. Campbell argues that the Aristaeus scenario
reflects the pattern of an initiation rite, similar to Isiac initiation described by
Apuleius. The myth of Orpheus is regarded as the climax of Aristaeus's initiation, the pinnacle of enlightenment. Crabbe shows that the Aristaeus episode
(or epyllion) subconsciously at the very least influenced Vergil during the
composition of Aeneid 5 and 6. The Palinurus story draws freely on the fates
of both Orpheus and Eurydice, and the preparation for Aeneas' catabasis
develops details from Aristaeus' visit to his mother, Cyrene, and from the
ultimate

in
