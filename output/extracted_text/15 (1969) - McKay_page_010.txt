Armstrong's article (61) provides a careful assessment of recent interpretations
of the epic. Michael Ayrton, the modern artist and sculptor (62), finds the
sources of his inspiration for repeated versions of Daedalus, Icarus, Sibyl, and
Minotaur in the combined influence of Berlioz, the numinous quality of the acropolis of Cumae, and the encouragement of Jackson Knight. Betts (63) juxtaposes
lines, images, and ideas used in common or in similar fashion by Shakespeare and
Vergil. Chapman's translation (65), with notes, is catastrophic. Christern (66)
examines the temple on the upper terrace of the Cumaean acropolis, originally
Greek, plundered (no doubt) and rebuilt by Augustus, ultimately transformed into
a Christian basilica. Collinge's remarks on ambiguity in literature (67) merit close
reading by critics of Vergil. Dale (68) comments on trochaic caesura in the third
foot, especially in Aeneid 6, 322-5, where it occurs in four consecutive lines. Doig
(69) explores proper names and local epithets with Greek roots or associations and
suggests that Vergil, with a proven interest in etymological associations, had wider
possibilities in mind when he selected Greek words or words with Greek associations.
Duckworth's article (70) on hexameter poetry puts Vergil into larger context. The
same scholar's study of the first foot dactyl comprising three words (71) suggests
that the higher frequency in the Georgics as opposed to the Aeneid may indicate
Lucretian influence on the didactic poems, or as a corollary, a desire to find
greater dignity and majesty for the epic by the reduction in the usage. Duckworth
(72) also shows, in a metrical study of Maphaeus Vegius' Thirteenth Book of the
Aeneid, published in 1428, that the Renaissance metric is basically dissimilar to
that of Vergil. D.C. Earl (73) treats 'nobilitas', 'virtus'
and 'gloria' as key words
and concepts in both Vergil and Horace. For Turnus, 'virtus' is linked with 'gloria',
