Mediaeval, Renaissance, Eighteenth Century and modern uses and translations of Vergil are prime subject-matter in the Caesarodunum volume edited
by Chevallier. Cadoni's article treating De compendiosa doctrina advances
variant readings for Vergil's works. Courcelle (194) probes the Christian interpretation of A. 7.338 mille nocendi artes and (195) the literary posterity of
A. 2.1-338. Franchey d'Espèrey treats the wounding of Silvia's stag as archetype for comparable incidents inStatius, Valerius Flaccus, and Sřlius Italicus.
Doignon finds reminiscences of Vergil and Seneca in Lactantius. Drake and
Forbes establish
that Vida's Christiad was token of the piety of the High
Renaissance, the metamorphosis of the Vulgate into a Vergilian counterpart,
a marriage of Classical and Biblical styles and forms. Ebeling-Koning studies
the influence of Vergil and Prudentius on Waltharius. Epstein's verses on
Virgil are noteworthy. Feo's article sheds light on Petrarch's doubts about the
Homeric and Vergilian Hades. Friedrich examines Servius ad Aen. 12.605 and
Mosella 316. Huebner studies G. 1.33-5 and Claudian. McGaha notes that
Cervantes' Don Quixote eliminated the flaws which diminished the credibility
of Vergil's epic: the reliance on magic, and divine machinery. Cervantes Christianized the Roman doctrine of fate and created a 'modern' hero, not the
plaster saint which mediaeval and Renaissance commentators had made of
Aeneas. Nazzaro argues that Pope Damasius used Vergil's Eclogue less
in
sympathy than in conflict. The ideology and literary values of Vergil were
adverse
to
Christian notions. Volta used
Lucretian and Vergilian tags
in
editing his scientific treatises, according to Pasoli. Poirion underscores the
ideological transformation of Vergil in the Roman d'Énéas. Allegory eased
the adaptation
to new political, social, and aesthetic conditions. Reinhold
examines Robert Lowell's distortion of Vergil's Second Aeneid; he assigns the
role of Aeneas to Ulysses as narrator of the fall of Troy. Magna pars fui, says
Vergil's hero; 'He had his part' says Lowell of Ulysses! Ricci studies a Vergilian cento from the standpoint of theme, structure, genre, meaning, and text.
Rudat's essays highlight the destruction of the epic poem and the epic hero.
Thompson notices a classical allusion (Quos ego . . .) in Chapter One of Flaubert's Madame Bovary. Susan Wiltshire explores the myth of Old Troy in the
American tradition: novels by Jo Reinhard Smith and Laura Krey, and works
by Allen Tate and Robert Penn Warren. Homecomings are paramount: 'the
homecoming is a problematic one . . . and as perilous as life is - but itis this
journey that defines Vergil's poem as the epic of civilization'.
