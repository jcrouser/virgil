Danovi suggests that the text of Donatus’s comment on Aen. 6.14–19
should probably read dum laudat conditorem or laudato conditore instead
of laudat conditor. Squillante Saccone cautions that the presence of legal
vocabulary in Donatus’s lexical repertoire does not necessarily imply that
he had a background in law. Only a few words belong to a purely technical
sphere, and in this material as a whole it is difficult to distinguish between
terms of generic postclassical usage and those of purely legal relevance.
Stok (2016–2017) provides an account of the use of the introductory
outline—the accessus—to Vergil’s work in Servius’s commentary, a form
that enjoyed a certain degree of popularity throughout the Middle Ages up
to the fourteenth century; Servius, in turn, had drawn on Aelius Donatus’s
preface to the Eclogues, which itself drew on forms of Theocritean exegesis,
as already attested in Pseudo-Probus. Tamás comments on Donatus’s
and Servius’s treatment of the Helen episode in Aen. 2. Tischer discusses
comparisons of Vergil’s and Cicero’s works in the commentary of Servius
and in Macrobius’s Saturnalia.
