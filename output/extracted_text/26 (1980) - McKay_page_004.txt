Paul Alpers' fresh reading of the bucolic poems accents the way the aesthetic concerns and pleasures of the poems derive from emotional, ethical, and
social realities. His interpretations of the poems apply to later works in the
same genre and to issues of poetics ingeneral. Coleiro 's life work (487 pages)
contains general considerations, critical essays on the several eclogues (3, 2, 5,
7, 9 & 1, 6, 4, 8, and 10), and text with apparatus criticus. The apparatus will
excite greatest
interest and comment. Division into three sections enables
Monsignor Coleiro to deal with readings from the main MSS which demand
serious attention and study; to record MSS readings which are more or less
obvious mistakes but which aid in the reconstruction of the history of the
text; and finally, to furnish evidence of ancient testimonia and of scholarship
throughout the ages to our own time. Coleiro provides an unusual bird's-eye
view of the history of the scholarship of the
text of the Bucolics. Thill
suggests
that Tityrus is indebted to Theocritus' Komatas (Idyll 7). Deryck
Williams' new text of the Eclogues and Georgics is marked by acute scholarship, uncanny sensitivity to sound and metre, and astute 'timely' judgment.
Scholars and
teachers will
be
forever in his debt: Macmillan has found a
worthy successor to the inimitable Page.
