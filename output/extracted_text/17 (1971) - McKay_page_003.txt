Bernard Dick's article (2) accents the real-ideal tension implicit
in the Arcadian
landscape, an ambiguity which splits into the persons of Meliboeus and Tityrus. Enthusiasm for Octavian and Rome is tempered by historical awareness (present exile) and
compassion. Skutsch (4) argues that the original form of EcL 2 excluded 45-55; he also
discovers a palindrome structure for the poem. Mrs. Griffiths' lecture (5) finds symbolic implications for myricae (Eel. 4.2): pastoral, prophecy, and Arcadia, a dream of
timeless ideals in an age of turbulence. Segal (6) identifies Chromis and Mnasyllus as
fauns or satyrs, not shepherds, who strengthen the mythical, unreal quality of the
setting where Silenus sings; there is a sense of suspended reality in the charmed setting
of the little drama. Elsewhere Segal (7) emphasizes the moral outlook EcL 6, discovers
an integral role for the proem (1-12), and a correlation between disorder in the universe
and evil within man. The Eclogue prefigures EcL 10 and the Aeneid where there is a
greater concern for man's capacity for destructive passion in love, and above all, in war.
Hardie (20) estimates the Georgics as a matrix of mythological themes first formed in
EcL 6. The themes recur in a framework of agricultural didactic in the Georgics and of
Greco-Roman history in the Aeneid. Spoerri's articles (9, 10) on the cosmogony of
Silenus' song provide useful explication of a complex passage in Ecl.6. Richter (1 1)
offers a thesis with text, translation, and commentary and a useful bibliography.
Dyer's botanical excursus (12) sheds light on Gallus' surrender to love as a rejection of
escapism and repression and a commitment to a new ambition. Garson's article (13) on
Theocritean elements in the Eclogues is a sensible corrective to preconditioned
approaches to the detection of influences. Lecrompe's index (14) will greatly assist
the mounting preoccupation with Vergil's earliest capo lavoro. Pavlovskis (15) studies
the originality of Vergil's attitude to nature and his humanization of nature as part
of his expectation of the advent of a Golden Age.
