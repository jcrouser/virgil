sacrificial overtones, some echo of the unum pro multis formula. Creusa,
Orontes, and Palinurus are sacrificial prelude to the symbolic descent of
Aeneas and to the awful violence ahead on Italian soil. Briggs studies Vergil's
design in employing repetitions to unite the Georgics and Aeneid by themes
and language. Dirckx deals with Aeneid 3.137-42 and Georgics 2.151-2, and
Aeneid 12.383-429
in
his medical study. Griffin attacks the validity of a
typological approach to the Aeneid. Holt treats dual perspective as a pervasive
feature of the epic. Rudd's memoir of T. S. Page, the Macmillan editor,
scholar, and controversialist, includes a comparison of Page's edition of Vergil
and Horace with those of forerunners and successors. Suerbaum's assessment
of Vergil's place in the classroom and seminar includes studies of the end of
the epic (46-103), and of the contest of Aeneas and Turnus and readers'
reactions (104-125); a select bibliography
is included (144-147). Deryck
Williams' presidential address to the British Classical Association considers the
multiplicity of Vergil's poetic abilities, surface and underlying qualities, the
sound factor and descriptive power of Vergil, his high-powered and intense
rhetoric, along with inner, deeper qualities of the poem.
