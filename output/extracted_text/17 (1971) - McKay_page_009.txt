Baxter (67) analyses three climatic passages in Tacitus' Histories, Book 3, and
discovers numerous Vergilian reminiscences grouped in too significant a fashion to be
fortuitous. There appears to be a conscious plan of a complex series of echoes from
Aeneid 2 which imparts a structural unity, a cotor Vergi/ianus, and another dimension
to the Sack of Cremona, Burning of the Capitol, and Death of Vitellius. Bruere (68)
traces Drances in Lucan, Silius Italicus, etc., including Milton's embodiment in Belial
(Paradise Lost 2, 108-1 16). Burck's review of Wilkinson's Georgics (69) is masterly
and helpful. Cairns (70) and Elliott (73) both examine the influence of Vergil on Les
Troyens of Berlioz with skill and insight, suggesting a new line of inquiry for Vergilian
scholars. D'Arms (72) illuminates the Vergilian terrain around Cumae and the Campi
Phlegraei. He consigns Vergil to Siro's villula at Neapolis ca. 37 B.C. Fauth (74) provides a comprehensive and original account of the underworld deities and demons
(e.g., Aen. 7., 445-61 ; 12,845-55; 5,654-63; 4,693-705, etc. Gransden (77, bis) defends
pastoral against charges of being trivial or insignificant; it provides insights into human
experience and human values which higher genres of poetry either ignore or treat by
borrowing from lower styles (satire and pastoral). Grülone (78) treats two different
Greek translations of A. 1, 649-51 and 702-7 preserved on papyri, and additional
instances in other papyri (79). Hardie (81) examines the mysterious antrum at Baiae
and dispels Vergilian acquaintance with the tunnel which was probably designed for
Orphic or other initiates during the tyranny of Aristodemus at Cumae (525-505 B.C.?).
Pope's Dunciad engages the attention of Kelsall (82) as criticism of Vergil's art.
Within the mock-heroic mode Kelsall finds latent criticism of Master Vergil. Klinz (83)
analyses the Atlas simile (Aen. 4, 245-51) and the shield (Aen. 8,617-25; 729-31) and
Aen. 12,818-41) in exemplary didactic fashion. Jackson Knight's remarks (84) on
spiritualistic survival, paranormal communication, and the eventual progression of
spirits from earth to a new-dimensional world, i.e., Elysium, are intensely personal and
contentious. Vergil, whose spirit Jackson Knight suggests was converted after death to
Christianity, plays a significant role in the argument. Day Lewis' lecture (85) ranges
from Sophocles to Vergil and modern poets. His remarks on the problems that confront translators of poetry are timely and stem from a rich and chequered experience.
