Odyssey and
Iliad portions. The bucolic aspect of the middle books
helps to diminish the epic-heroic scale and portentous atmosphere; there
is instead, an easy familiarity, a world of dreams, delusions, and pastoral
and rural elements reminiscent of the Eclogues and Georgics. Wimmel's
brief study (21 pages) merits the closest attention by scholars. Tanner
(66) favours an allegorical interpretation of the last six books of the epic.
Convinced that the political and military events connected with Mutina,
Philippi, Perusia and Actium lurk beneath the poetic lines, Tanner finds
Cicero the pattern for Evander and Pallas, Catiline for Cacus, Maecenas
for Tarchon. Nisus and Euryalus reflect Hirtius and Pansa (a counteipart
discovered earlier in my Vergil*s Italy
p.45); Achates is a rather tightlipped reflection of Agrippa. Behind Drances there lies neither Cicero
nor Maecenas, whose names have been preferred earlier, but L. Munatius
Plancus. The impact of contemporary politics on the poet has rarely
been argued so vigorously. Latinus-Lepidus, Lavinia-Servilia, AmataServilia the Elder, mother of Brutus, will haunt the imagination far more
disturbingly than Octavian-Aeneas or Antony-Turnus. Binder (67) offers
the best available interpretation of Aen. 8 so far available. He provides
a grand explication de texte, a line-by-line analysis of the book, with
heavy emphasis (133 pages) on the shield. This model of detailed scholarship will do much to remedy popular disinterest in the last six books
of the epic. Galinsky (68) advances Plato, Euthyd. 297C, to illuminate
Aen. 8, 299-30. Plato's equation of the Hydra with cleverness and reason
brings Hercules into a philosophical context. Vergil's rationis egentem
indicates that only a rational hero can prevail over the Hydra's dangers.
So Hercules emerges even more clearly as a worthy model for Aeneas.
