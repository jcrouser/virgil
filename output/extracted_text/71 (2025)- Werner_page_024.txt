Abbamonte and Kallendorf trace the origins of indexing up to the printed
editions of the Italian humanist Lorenzo Valla’s (1407–1457) Elegantie
and Niccolò Perotti’s (1429–1480) Cornucopiae, as well as early modern
editions of Vergil. Danovi (2022) discusses the ninth-century MSS Bern,
Burgerbibliothek, 172 and 167, containing the Vergilian commentary
known as the Scholia Bernensia. Danovi (2023a) has discovered that at least
some of Donatus’s notes in the manuscript Bern, Burgerbibliothek, 165 (=
Tu, the so-called Vergil of Tours) seem to be based on the manuscript
Città del Vaticano, Biblioteca Apostolica Vaticana, Reg. Lat. 1484 (= R).
Since the analogous scholia are not present in the manuscript Città del
Vaticano, Biblioteca Apostolica Vaticana, Vat. Lat. 1570 (= Va), it remains
unclear whether they were already present in the common archetype of
Tu and Va (= τ). The position of R also remains unclear. Danovi (2023b)
points out that it is commonly assumed that Donatus’s commentary on
Vergil was still available to Carolingian scholars in the ninth century.
The proof was seemingly provided by J. J. H. Savage (De scholiis in
Turonensi Virgili codice scriptis, 1923, and later writings). An examination
of the manuscripts, however, shows that Savage’s position does not
stand up to scrutiny and that the terminus post quem for the loss of
Donatus’s commentary must be set at least a century earlier. Fratantuono
conjectures aletur in place of the transmitted agetur at Aen. 1.574. Laneri
discusses the fourteenth-century codex Alghero, Biblioteca Comunale
“Rafael Sari,” 58, which contains the Aeneid, other Vergilian material,
Dante’s Epistle 7 and Petrarch’s Epistle 2.5; and offers a transcription of
two anonymous poems. Mañas Núñez studies the miscellaneous notes by
Spanish humanist Francisco Sánchez de las Brozas (1523–1600) found at
the end of his commentary on the Eclogues. These aim to demonstrate that
the Aeneid contains numerous interpolations: in addition to fifty-eight
incomplete, unfinished, or truncated verses, the humanist found at least
twenty more verses that, in his opinion, should be expurgated. Morales
