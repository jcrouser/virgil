Vergil revived the Theocritean dramatic form
and
Arcadian setting but
largely eliminated the exuberance and fervour of subjective love-poetry
in an idealized world of song.
The passion
and
the vicissitudes
of
elegiac love poetry were, in Vergil's view, anomalous in the pastoral
tranquillity of his new Arcadia.
Eclogue 10, to Cornelius Gallus, helps
one to demarcate the two poetic forms and moods and reminds one of
the pastoral break with passion.
