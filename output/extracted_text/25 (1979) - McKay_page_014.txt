Pearce shows that V. is indebted to Theocritus for the conventional
behaviour attaching to gifts. Eclogue 2, 31-44, reveals the various aspects of
the theme extended and is the culminating example of the practice. Schlunk
deals with the Dolonia (Iliad 10) and the Nisus-Euryalus episode (A. 9) to
show how V.
used and considered ancient commentaries in adapting the
Homeric episode to his Night Episode. There are links between Asios (Iliad)
and Nisus (Aeneid): both are paradigms of death through disobedience and
self-confidence. Tracy detects Cat. 101 and 46 inAeneid 6. 333-6. The double borrowing reflects conflicting emotions inAeneas:
the finality of death
and loss, and the joyful return of spring. Aen. 6. 692 reveals another reference to Cat.
101 in a book closely associated with death, separation, and
farewell.
