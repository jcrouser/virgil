impact of Vergil on 19th century Germany, England, France, and Italy. Longfellow's conception of Nova Scotia as a New Troy is a token of the influence and
eternity of Vergil. C. P. Segal's essay on Classics and Comparative Literature is
required reading for any programmer or exponent. Shankman provides a critique of
an anti-pastoral pastoralist who adopts details and precedents from Vergil. Suerbaum
responds favorably to the cycle of illustrations provided by Barry Moser to the
second edition of Mandelbaum's translation of the epic. Verneťs concise account of
Vergil, from the 6th to the 15th century, explores the posthumous life of the poet,
anthologies, imitations of the Eclogues , Georgics, and Aeneid and the high reputation
of the poet's genius. Vidal provides an autopsy of the Vergilian cento which merges
Christian pastoral with doctrinal and theological matters. Wiltshire studies affinities
between the Kentucky poet and the Mantuan, and points to Vergil's influence on
Allen Tate's three major poems: "The Mediterranean," "Aeneas at Washington," and
"Aeneas at New York," and on his only novel, The Fathers.
