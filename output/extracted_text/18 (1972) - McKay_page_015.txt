for the nineteenth century versions Sandbach has no favourable comment.
He singles out Patric Dickinson (1961), Rolfe Humpries (1951), the late
C. Day Lewis, and Jackson Knight for special comment among the moderns. Jocelyn Toynbee (151) is less concerned with beliefs about the
afterlife than with burial practices, but there are many illuminating areas
in her study which will affect Vergilian studies. Augustus' cylindrical
mausoleum in the Campus Martius (28 ВС) was almost certainly influenced
by Etruscan patterns at Cerveteri etc. Just as the Augustan architect
responded to visual history so too the Augustan pro-Etruscan policy was
another recreation of the past. The implication of this retrospective
attitude cannot be lost on students on Vergil. Zanker's study of the
sculptural details of the Augustan Forum (160) has important implications for study of the structure of the Aeneid and for the poet's use
of artworks throughout. Verdière (156) includes the Ecologues and Dido
in his lecture notes. Wigodsky (159) expands his Princeton doctoral dissertation (1964) into a profound study of the impact of early Latin poetry
on Vergil. Considerable attention is given to Livius Andronicus, Naevius,
Ennius (40-79), Pacuvius and Accius (80-97), et al. Scholars will appreciate
his epilogue on Catullus and Lucretius (126-39), and on Vergil and Horace
(147-8). Gunther Zuntz (161) is not concerned directly with the impact
of South Italian cult and belief on Vergil but the implications are legion.
