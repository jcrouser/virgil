Camps' lecture on Aeneid I is a perceptive study of the design and content.
Fredricksmeyer's article attacks the poem's primary historical meaning
in the propositio (1-7) and invocatio (8-11) where thematic and programmatic attitudes and values are sounded. The thematic issues are (1) the principle of teleology, and (2) the problem of theodicy. Both motifs in combination are, as Fredericksmeyer reminds us, unprecedented in classical literature. Gross explores the transformations of gaza
from A.
1.119 and 2.763-4,
to 5.40 (gaza . . . agresti). Harrison's bimillenary lecture explores the adventitious appearances of Mercury in the epic. Knox detects a recasting of aspects
of the Argonautica and the Odyssey in obstipuit primo aspectu Sidónia Dido.
Sarkissian assesses
the storm
of Aeneid 1 as a reflection of the political
atmosphere of the late Republic; Aeolus is seen as a trespassing magistrate,
guilty of violating impérium. The sequel, the sedition simile, and Jupiter's
prophecy of Rome's Augustan domination (257-96), strengthens the political
'reading.' Gransden's articles on Troy's fall explore language and style, adaptation from the cyclic Little Iliad, the non-Vergilian character of the Helen
episode, and analogies between Sinon and Iago. Hardie traces repeated applications of motifs from the Lucretian sacrifice of Iphigenia in the Sinon episode (A.
2.57-198), the Death of Laocoon (A. 2.199-231), and the omen
attaching to Jůlus (A. 2.679-91). Zintzen's monograph enriches the understanding of the Laocoon episode. Basto's study of weaponry in A. 4 provides
fresh insights into the Dido and Aeneas episode. Farron demonstrates that
Vergil's contemporaries were much more sentimental, romantic, and emotional than people today. She also treats the startling oxymoron "she has
been watching him without looking at him" (R. D. Williams, ad. loc.). Farron
rejects 'hostile' (A. 6.469 underscores her refusal to interact - 'with her back
towards him'), and explores the connotations of aversa as an extreme instance of Vergilian ambiguity and compression, initially incomprehensible.
