The Scholia Bernensia constitute one of five principal extant studies from
antiquity of Vergil's Eclogues. The anonymous collection of notes follows the
form and content of Philargyrius' Explanationes. Although there are game
attempts to preserve the more traditional interpretations of Vergil's poems,
allegorical interpretations are also manifest.

The English poets and T. S. Eliot claim the major attention. Kelsall (73)
examines Trapp's translation of the>Aeneid (1718, 1720) with its extensive
preface and elaborate notes, the best known evaluation of Vergil after Dryden's version. Trapp saw the architecture of the epic as so intricately complex
that its order defied rational explanation. For Trapp, Vergil (and Milton) was
as passionate and as perfect as Bernini and Borromini. Donker (74) indicates
that Eliot evokes the concatenation of events inA. 1-6 inhis Waste Land. He
reformulates this
literary monument of the past as a commentary on the
present and echoes episodes already ingrained inthe literary tradition (Dante,
Spenser, and Milton). Images of The Waste Land have conspicuous analogues
in the Aeneid. Anchises' sermon inA. 6, a recapitulation of Aeneas' own
purgatorial experience as quester, suggests the movement of The Waste Land
as a series of trials by water, wind, and fire. Vergil isboth guide and inspiration for Eliot. Perutelli's study (76) of traditional aspects inEnglish criticism
of Vergil is aligned with Eliot's verdict on the poet. Segal (77) finds Rilke's
version of the Orpheus-Eurydice episode a radical alteration of the original.
