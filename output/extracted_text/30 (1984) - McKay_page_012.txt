offers useful insights into Vergil's philosophical inclinations and tenets. Epicureanism is the hallmark of both the
Bucolics and the Georgics for both Grilli and Grimal. Nielson argues that
Aeneas must, according to religious convention, keep his obligations to the
dead. In the case of Mago and Turnus, where the claims of the living are
involved, Aeneas' pietas has to override sympathy. Novara argues that there is
a Socratic tradition implicit in the expression - felix qui potuit rerum cognosces causas (Georgics 2.490) implying a doctrine of happiness based on the
knowledge of causes. Webb detects a consistent scheme in the recurrence of
direct contacts involving the hero and divinities during the epic. There is an
evolving process of response on the hero's part and a system underlies the
supernatural interventions which is almost programmatic in character.
