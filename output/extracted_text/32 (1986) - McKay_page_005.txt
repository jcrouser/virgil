Gazich and Hering are both concerned with Eclogue 1; Hering's review of
Eclogue 1 appears in pp. 184-207. Van Sickle provides valuable instruction in his
"reading" of the first Eclogue , and contests Ian DuQuesnay's strictures in Papers ,
Liverpool Latin Seminar 3 (1981) 29-182. The troublesome riddles of Damoetas (E.
3, 104f) and Menalcas (106f) are studied by Hofmann with reference to Aratus'
Phainomena. Beaujeu despairs of an identification for the Wunderkind and settles for
a new generation, an anonymous child of the Roman aristocracy. Bevilacqua recognizes divination and possibly Hebraic influence in the Messianic Eclogue but stresses
the GrecoRoman traits of history, work, and peace. Monteleone favors Lactantius
(Div. inst. 7, 24, 9-11) and pre-fifth century writers who follow a text providing:
18-20, 23, 26-7, 31-41, 28, 30, 21-22, and 24-5. The net result is a sophisticated
genethliakon rather than prophecy. Knox examines the "intensely Callimachean"
setting of the Sixth Eclogue and provides a parallel for Gallus' initiation in the antiCallimachean, Antimachus of Colophon.

Boscherini highlights Pythagorean science in the Georgics in a most suggestive fashion. Muecke argues that Vergil attempted to reconcile the disparity between
the moral earnestness of Lucretius' didactic poem with the technical material of the
Georgics. Wilhelm traces solar associations for both Julius and Augustus Caesar. The
ties between Sol and Apollo and Augustus were token of fusion's energy and order
in the world.
