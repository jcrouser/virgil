The song of lopas, according to Kinsey, contains no symbolism, although
it plays an
important role
in
the development of the Aeneas-Dido
relationship. There
is also no attempt to arouse Roman prejudice against the
Carthaginians. Gaunt examines the bardic song of lopas which, inhis view,
fits the occasion, and suits the grandeur of the epic. The Cognys find a
rhetorical association for the motif of the severed-head on the river bank or
shore-line. Kenney's critical study of the similes in Aen. 2.469-505 introduces
Homer, Nicander, Lucretius, and the Georgics. He also explores the encircling
structures of 469-500 and the problem of ambiguity attaching to limen and
postes in 500-505. Zurli treats the Celaeno prophecy (A. 3.253-7). Farron
argues that Vergil deliberately portrays Aeneas' mission as brutal and destructive inAen. 4. Heuzé compares the agony of Dido and Camilla and comments
on the composition of the epic. Breaks with the oriental past and progress
towards a Roman future are, for Holt, the main theme of the Fifth Aeneid.
Bollók finds the inspiration for A. 6 inan Orphic Catabasis which is indebted
to the Egyptian Book of the Dead. Vergil may have discovered his model in
Epicurean polemics against popular beliefs. Martin examines A. 6.440-444,
and 450-66. Grimal studies A. 8.345-6, 347, 313. Monteleone is concerned
with the invocation to the Muse at the start of the lliadic section of the epic.
Erato introduces the impending epithalamium (Aeneas & Lavinia) and follows
neoteric patterns. Schilling is concerned with the Kabeiroi of Samothrace.
