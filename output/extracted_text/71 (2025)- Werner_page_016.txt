Holzberg observes that Vergil, Horace, Tibullus, and Propertius display
conspicuous restraint when talking about or even alluding to Octavian
in their poetry written in the thirties and twenties of the first century
BCE. Octavian seems to be featured in early Augustan poetry as deus
absconditus and only later, in poems composed after the Aeneid, expressly
revered as Σωτήρ. Evidence that supports this is offered from texts that
predate the Aeneid, including Vergil, Ecl. 4 and 8; Horace, Serm. 1.5;
Tibullus 1.7, and others. O’Rourke considers Propertius’s final collection
of elegies as the earliest concerted response to the poetic career of Vergil
in its totality. The analysis reveals an elegiac Vergil as much as it does an
epicizing Propertius. Putnam focuses on the multivalent appearances of
the concept of pietas in Horace and Vergil. Santos discusses passages from
the Georgics and from Columella, Res rustica 9 which refer to the illness
and death of bees.
