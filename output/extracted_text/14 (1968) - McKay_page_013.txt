Benario (80) has examined the affinity of Tacitus for Vergil, regarding the historian
as bard of Vergil's dream perverted, and Miss Bews (81) has concentrated at greater
length on the nature and significance of Vergilian influence on the language and
themes of the Annals. Brisson (82) is concerned with Vergil's life and offers specific
dates which will appeal to some, not to others:
e.g., 53-50 B.C., Vergil at Rome
attending the school of Epidius; 49 B.C., at Naples, with Siro; October, 45 B.C.,
return to Mantua; 43-41 B.C., composed Eclogues
2,3,5,7, at Mantua; 38 B.C.,
Vergil established in Campania (Geòrgie 1); 37 B.C., Eclogue 10 and publication of
the bucolic collection. Vergil's literary influence on authors other than Tacitus has
attracted several scholars, always with profit to the strait-jacketed Latinist (83, 85,
90, 91, 95, 97, 101, 108). D'Avanzo (85) argues that Keats' first descent of Endymion (Book 2) owes largely to Aeneid 6; Endymion's model is Aeneas and his
descent follows Aeneas' pattern very closely. Hudson-Williams (90) finds Vergilian
influence in the poetry of the late Latin period, the 4th and 5th centuries particularly. Lucan's indebtedness to Vergil is carefully reviewed by Thompson and Bruere
(108) and more imaginatively though incompletely by Morford (97). Juvenal's debt
to Vergil has been partially explored by Joly (95) but much remains to be done.
The reprint of the Platnauer Fifty Years of Classical Scholarship is disappointing
in a number of respects, not least in the failure to up-date Catullus and The Augustan Poets. Jocelyn's (92) study of Ennius' tragedies involves Vergil's use of Alexander and Medea. The reprint of E.K. Rand's Magical Art of Virgil (102) is enterprising and gratifying. Two collections of importance to the overall study of Vergil
are Swanson's word list (106) and Thompson's bibliography of American Ph.D.
dissertations (107). Schoder's (104) assessment of the portraiture of Vergil is perceptive but incomplete since it lacks Gisela Richter's recently identified versions
which are totally different from the Villa Medici relief type and the Bardo mosaic.
Williams, with customary insight, lauds Servius' commentary as a monumental work
of enormous erudition (on the whole) and solid common sense. Imaginative insights
and sensitive literary criticism are not ingredients of his encyclopedic antiquarian
commentary.
