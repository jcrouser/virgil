Williams' Presidential address to
the Virgil Society explores reasons for
Vergil's lasting popularity: his variety of poetic mood and style, his ability to
juxtapose different aspects of human behaviour so as to show sympathy for
widely contrasting attitudes of life. Contemporary appreciation realizes the
many-sidedness of the poet's genius and qualities. Segal (43) deals with a
recurrent motif inA. 2, 790-95; 5, 738-40, and 6, 700-702. Earlier usage is
intensified, and there is an evident progression, an artistic maturity, in the
movement from tangible aspects to a more evocative, fugitive event. Luck
(44) restudies Warburton's suggestion (1837) that Aeneas' descent recalls the
procedures, stages, and 'illumination' of initiation into the Eleusinian Mysteries.
Orphic
elements,
detected
by
others,
were
assimilated
into
the
Mysteries and are entirely relevant to the Vergilian descent. Luck's article
brings fresh attention to the richness and complexity of Vergil's art. Oroz
Reta (45) treats political aspects of Roman religion, ritual, cult of the dead,
sacrificial practices, gods and heroes, and Magnus Saeclorum Ordo; a comprehensive,
useful study. Cupaiuolo (47) examines the political and historical
background of Vergil's writings. Van Rooy (48) argues that Horace borrowed
the concept of an organised book of 10 satires, a bipartite division marked by
two introductory poems (1, and 6), and imitated themes and expressions of
(mainly) the corresponding number in the E-book. (Satires 1, 2, 4, 7, 8, 9,
10).
