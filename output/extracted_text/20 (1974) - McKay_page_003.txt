Eleanor Leach's volume is concerned with landscape and the relationship
of landscape to the myth of natural freedom. (Both volumes are properly
concerned with the poet's 'place' of inspiration and creation but the arguments and the conclusions differ.) Mrs. Leach rejects the oft-stated interpretation of hostility between the pastoral and the Roman worlds. Theocritan
models notwithstanding, Vergil's poems are invested with a distinctive Roman
atmosphere and symbolism. Cato, Varro, and Vitruvius shed helpful light on
Vergil's portrayals of innocence and landscape, and on his poetic imagination.
Landscape paintings are adduced skilfully and persuasively
to support and
enlarge
the argument. Vergil's pastoral landscapes emerge as symbolic patterns, as poetic compositions which incorporate images of the natural world.
And behind the landscapes resides a philosophy, a personal estimate of man's
condition and of his place inthe environment. Vergil's Corydon and Damon
seek escape into a world of unspoiled innocence; Vergil ismore resolute and
abides with the world of experience, and with the complexity of contemporary civilization.
Pearce's dissertation provides a thematic (or motif) index to the corpus of
ancient bucolic poetry. Mette aligns the puer of E. 4 with the child of Octavia
and Antony, a child with traits of both Antony and Octavian.
