THE AENEID: CHARACTERS
60. John Arthur Hanson, 'Aeneas/ Eight Essays in Classical Humanities
(ed. G. S. Schwartz) (Milburn, New Jersey, 1975) 59-69.
61. Grace Starry West, Women in
Vergil's Aeneid. Diss., University of
California, Los Angeles, 1975. Summary inDA 36 (1975) 2800A-2801 A.
Hanson's essay examines aspects of Aeneas which seem to impede his
mission and contradict Jupiter's optimism. A comparison of Aeneas with
Odysseus discloses marked differences between the heroes: in self-control,
intellectual
awareness,
and
sympathy
with
women.
Aeneas,
for Hanson,
shows no maturation or change: he remains 'a man who lacks self-control,
who cannot comprehend reality, and who cannot deal with the feminine half
of human life.'

Binder's encyclopaedia article examines the Aeneas legend from Homeric

THE AENEID: GEOGRAPHY AND ARCHAEOLOGY
65. B. Conticello, rev. G. Saeflund, The Polyphemus and Scylla Groups at
Sperlonga, Gnomon 47 (1975) 686-92.
66. J. Rufus Fears, 'Cumae in the Roman Imperial Age,' Vergilius 21
(1975) 1-21 (15 plates.)
67. W. Fuchs, 'Die Bildgeschichte der Flucht des Aeneas', Aufstieg und
Niedergang der röm. Weit I,4 (1973) 615-32 (32 plates).
Fears studies the Roman monuments on the Cumaean acropolis and inthe
Forum area. The common assertion that Cumae was a ghost town by Juvenal's time is clearly mistaken; Flavian and Antonine rulers infused new life
and imparted a new monumentality to the venerable site. The Daedalic marvels of legend become a fresh
reality. Fuchs compiles a rich portfolio of
artworks relating to Aeneas' evacuation from Troy.
VERGIL'S LIFE AND WORKS
68. William R. Nethercut, 'Lord of the Scales,' CO 53 (1976) 51-4.
69.
Michael
Winterbottom,
'Virgil
and
the Confiscations,' G&R
23
(1976) 55-9.
Nethercut
notes
the
coincidence
of Saturn's aspects
with
important
periods and events in the
lives of Julius Caesar, Cicero, Augustus, Vergil,
Horace, and Ovid. Vergil was born with the Sun in Libra and the Moon in
Sagittarius, a combination significant for his life and his creativity. Winterbottom's restrained, uncomplicated review of the evidence for Vergil's displacement is welcome.
