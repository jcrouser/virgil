captures the poet's intention. Boyle (77) ignores biographical
postulates and historical contexts in his study of the meaning of the epic.
He treats the ideology-reality dichotomy, the disparity between the ideal
ofempire and the practice as reflected by Vergil, and the disparity between
achievement and cost, which is the pervasive, arresting theme of the
poem. Boyle is impressed with the constellation of thematically related
images of serpent, fire, wound, and storm. He concludes Part I of his
study with the commonplace of Vergilian criticism in the 60' s and 70' s:
'Vergil's concern (is) to emphasize that it is a victory for the forces
of non-reason and the triumph not of pietas but of furor.' Glenn (79)

counterparts in an age of revolution. Lenz (85) examines 58 hemistichs
