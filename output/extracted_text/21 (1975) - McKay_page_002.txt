VERGILIAN BIBLIOGRAPHY (1974-75)
The present survey of Vergilian scholarship
is the thirteenth in the Vergilius series. Classical World 68.1 (1974), published in association with the
Vergilian
Society,
Inc.,
contains
my 'Recent
Work
on
Vergil:
A
Bibliographical Survey, 1964 - 1973.' The Survey may be obtained from the
offices of Classical World and from the Treasurer of The Vergilian Society,
Inc. A list oí Addenda to the ten-year survey appeared as a Supplementum to
'Vergilian Bibliography (1973-74)' in Vergilius 20 (1974). The editor of the
"Vergilian Bibliography" welcomes reprints of articles and bibliographical
notices for inclusion in subsequent bibliographies. The mailing address is:
Department of Classics, McMaster University, Hamilton, Ontario L8S 4M2.
APPENDIX VERGILIANA
1. Cipriano Conti, 'Rassegna di studi suli' Appendix Vergiliana (Il Parte),
BStudLat 4 (1974) 229-63.
2. W. R. Nethercut, 'Weaving: A Point of Art in the C/r/s,' CB 51 (1975)
62.
3. J.A. Richmond, 'The Archtype of the Priapea and Cataiepton, ' Hermes
102 (1974) 300-304.
Conti's
article
completes
his
annotated
survey
of
scholarship
from
1955-72. Nethercut argues that compounds of texere, "to weave", lend unity
to
the epyllion. Richmond supports the independence of the four Priapea
from the sixteen poems of Cataiepton.
ECLOGUES: GENERAL STUDIES
4. Javier de Echave-Sustaeta, 'Virgilio desde dentro: dos claves de estilo en
las 'Eglogas", Estudios clásicos 17 (1973) 261-90.
5.
E.
Flintoff, 'The Setting of Virgil's Eclogues,' Latomus 33 (1974)
814-46.
6. Gilbert Highet, 'Performances of Vergil's Bucolics' Vergilius 20(1974)
24-5.
7. E. D. Kollmann, 'Die Stimme Vergiis inseinen Eklogen,' StudCias 15
(1973) 69-85.
Echave-Sustaeta treats echo and resonance and the shift from reality to the
imaginary
as
poetic
characteristics
of
the Eclogues.
Flintoff accepts
the
Bucolics as a cycle of poems with a deep underlying unity. The spiritual
landscape, Arcadia, is set in the real landscape of Northern Italy; the simple
place-names often
have great complexity of meaning. Higheťs article is a
reminder that Vergil's bucolic poems were performed or recited publicly and
suggests performing casts of cantores.
ECLOGUES: INDIVIDUAL POEMS
8. B. Lewis, 'Time and Tense. The central problem inVirgil's first eclogue,'
CB 50 (1974) 59-63.
9. M. Bettini, 'Corydon Corydon,' St.Ciass.Orientali 21 (1972) 261-76.
10.
Jenny Strauss Clay, 'Damoetas' Riddle and the Structure of Vergil's
Third Eclogue,' Phiiologus 118 (1974) 59-64.
