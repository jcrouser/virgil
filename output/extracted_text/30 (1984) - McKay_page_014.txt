Adams detects that Vergil avoids using unpoetic words for body parts and
sidesteps mentioning undignified parts and organs. Unlike Homer, Vergil seldom describes wounds, specific organs, or facial features. Boyd treats Eclogue
2.51, Corydon's gift to Alexis of 'sparrow-quinces,' and explores the ramifications of Cydon and Cydonius in connection with the fruit and passim.
There are amusing associations of love-tokens and arrows that resonate in the
usages. Huxley notes that the adjective Saturnius is made emphatic by its
unusual position, before the second-foot diaeresis at Aeneid 4.372. Lesueur's
treatment of the noun and verb for motion is instructive. Thomas' study of
expositions
of art works deals
with Eclogue 3.36-48 (cups), and Aeneid
8.675-77 (Aeneas' shield). The centrepiece of the shield (8.675-7) is also
central to the description. Usually, in fact, Vergil's poem reflects the art work
described* (cf. Geo. 3.16 in medio Caesar,and the murals in Juno's Temple at
Carthage, (Aeneid 1.466-93). Thomas provides incentive for further study of
the ekphrasis
along just such lines, in terms of the linguistic and dictional
tradition.
Wilhelm studies the image
of the chariot and its mastery as an
epitome of the qualities and skills associated with leadership. For Vergil the
chariot involves both personal and political control, the latter associated not
only with the Roman impérium but also with Augustus himself.
