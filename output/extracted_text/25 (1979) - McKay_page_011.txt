Barrett provides a catalogue of allusions inRoman Britain to Vergil's epic:
Silchester; coins of Carausius; Low Ham, Somerset; Oxford, Kent; Frampton,
Dorset; and Lullingstone, Kent. The triclinium inthe last (ca. AD 330) exhibits a mosaic picture of the abduction of Europa with an inscribed couplet
referring to A.
1. The couplet, in Barrett's view, also bespeaks knowledge of
Ovid in fourth century Britain. Benario also offers evidence of a fairly detailed acquaintance with Vergil's text among the local gentry. Benario's article on the Tiber explores V's use of water imagery with frequent associations of terror and hopelessness; storm and the concomitant darkness are
besetting problems inthe epic. Tiber is the instrument of change of fortune, a
change which forecasts the future and foretokens eventual success. Two Appendices list appearances of Tiber in7-12 and examine V's words for water.
Green suggests that originally Icarus was probably associated with lcaria-byMarathon, and that Daedalus' landing at Cyme was originally on the Euboean
coastline, looking towards Scyros. Paratore's article deals with the Cumaean
locales
(temple,
grotto),
Sibylline associations, and
Avernus as portal
to

Paratore is more
interested in legendary and cult matters than in
topography and existing monuments. Préaux brings the somewhat contentious finds at Lavinium into the context of the prelude to the epic. Tilly
explores the Ager Capenas (A. 7. 695-7) and Lucus Feroniae. She concludes
that V's mention of the Cápenos lucos 'was inconcert with the revivifying of
ancient Italic cults so dear to Augustus, with the existence of the annual fair
and the wide repute of Feronia ...
the colony was already established and
living in his time.' (53) Ferdinando Castagnoli treats the Campi Phlegraei in
expert fashion, with emphasis on earlier accounts and illustrators (Sangallo,
Paoli et al.). Air photographs, revised maps, and succinct background commentary shed light on the Portus Julius, Puteoli, Baiae, Misenum, and Cumae.
