Janice Benario (24) compares and contrasts Vergil's Dido with Horace's Cleopatra. Similarity in choice of words and expressions is hardly coincidental and Vergil's
Carthaginian queen shows familiarity with Cleopatra's characteristics and Horace's
portrayal. Janet Bews (25) detects flaws in Duckworth's thesis regarding the Golden
Section, and argues for a controlled rather than a rigid structure straitjacketed by a
formula. Clark's article (27) examines an amusing parallel between Bishop Fleming's
isolation of terrain for a cathedral in St. John's Newfoundland and Dido's foundation
of Carthage (Aen. 1.367-8). Stegen (29) offers an analysis of the structure and content
of the contested verses preceding arma virumque. Constance Bullock-Davies (30)
argues that the tragedy of Troy was conceived within a context of gates, doors, lintels,
and thresholds, worthy examples of Vergil's epic originality. Goold's study (31) of
the Helen episode is detailed and enlightening. Characteristics include use of the
Golden Section, Vergilian metrical technique, and effective use of rhetoric and drama
(Ennius and Euripides). Goold argues that the oft-disputed passage was composed
before 65 A.D. (cf. Lucan, Phars. 10.53ff) by an anonymous author. Harrison (32)
respects the common acceptance of the Helen passage as Vergilian and considers how
Vergil came to compose the episode. Lundström (33) favours furenti (770) rather
than ruenti and discards Austin's reservations about the alliteration in sine fine furenti.
