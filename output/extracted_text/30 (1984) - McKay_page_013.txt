D'Anna studies early responses to Vergil's output and argues that neither
Horace nor Propertius was a fervent admirer of the early Vergil. Fairweather
regards the Qasr Ibrim papyrus, evidently reflecting Gallan verse, as a fragment of an amoebean song-contest. Signs separating the quatrains indicated
where the text of the contest-poem called for a change of singer. The precedent for elegiacs in a singing match exists in (Theocr.) Idyll 8.33-60. Gagé
examines the literary taste and writing of Augustus; the article offers a mine
of useful references. Hardie stresses Horace's indebtedness to Vergil, Georg.
3.1 Off.in the celebrated Odes, 3.30; Horace's linking of poet as triumpbator
with an architectural metaphor, his hope for public recognition, the references to Greek models, to his home town and native river, and the new
imperial vision are common factors. Heyworth suggests that the Gallus fragment does not derive from a continuous poem. The first verse belongs properly to another work; both are components of an anthology of verse, maybe
not exclusively Gallan. Huzar's survey of Antony's stabs at literature contains
a rich collection of references and sheds interesting side-lights on Vergil's
poetry. Philipps' rethinking of Augustan poetry, content, design, and propaganda, deserves wide reading and continuing reflection by Vergilian scholars.
Quinn's reminder that publication for the Augustans was more recitatio and
acroasis than written texts is useful. His treatment of art and society (166-7),
the collegium poetarum,and other matters is interesting and enlightening and
the article is a useful companion to his Martin Classical Lecture (1975), and
to his Texts and Contexts: The Roman Writers and their Audience (Toronto
1979).
