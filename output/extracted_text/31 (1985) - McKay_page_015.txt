Battegazzore's study of the imagery of fire recapitulates much of Bernard
Knox's incendiary original, and provides fresh material and ideas outside
Aeneid 2. Chênerie's piece on the hexameter is highly instructive. Conte's
diatribe cautions against too much emphasis on echoes and reverberations of
language and on comparative study in Vergilian criticism. Evidence of his own
approach appears in an analysis of A. 10.873-908 (the death of Mezentius)
and of A. 10.540-42, the killing of Haemon. Dangel's articles provide helpful
and directional accounts of her painstaking study of the verse lines. Görler's
bimillenary lecture at Cumae
offers a careful analysis of Vergil's syntax,
emphasizing the way in which the poet displaces adjectives to modify nouns
in unusual contexts. Herein resides the essential art and unique force of
Vergil's lines. La Penna's article on tragic dawns has perennial value. Oksala's
study of Graecisms in the Bucolics merits close reading and development. Ott
continues
to provide useful metrical analyses of Aeneid 5 and 7. Williams
