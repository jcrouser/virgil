Cairns examines the poles of Discord and Concord around which the epic
moves. The Aeneiďs first simile (1.148-54) Cairns associates with Marc Antony's
discord. Cattiverà underlines Vergil's concern for Italy, united and consolidated by
Rome. Cleary's review article continues to offer useful counsel to high-school
teachers. Colucci examines the movement of stars in A. 2.9; 2.250; 6.539; and 8.369.
Segal's translation includes the larger part of Conte's two earlier volumes, both well
received: Memoria dei poeti e sistema letterario (1974) and II genere e i suoi confini
(1980, 1984). Particularly valuable are Conte's interpretation of Eclogue 10,
Aristaeus, Orpheus and the Georgics , Vergil's Aeneid , the Baldric of Pallas, and the
Helen Episode. Dauge argues for astronomical symbolism in the 12 books of the
epic, both solar and stellar. Hardie's new volume explores cosmology and
imperialism in the epic. Harrison's prodigies include the horse's head (1, 443-9) and
the Table Eating prophecy. Hauck's dissertation deals with the temple wall decorations at Carthage, and the temple doors at Cumae, and accents Vergil's originality
and psychological insights in the descriptions. Jenkyns argues forcefully for a more
detached view of Vergilian pessimism and melancholy; a thoughtful, contentious and
spirited plea for reassessment of contemporary views which so often abandon hope
as a Vergilian trait. Lyne's study of pathos includes A. 4.494-7, 646-53; and
12.45 Iff, 473ff. Muecke considers the peculiar force of Dido's gestures of rejection.
Newbold's study of sensitivity to shame samples the first 250 verses from all the
epic books. Perotti's Latin article includes the slaughter of Priam, Dido and Anna,
Dido and Cleopatra, and Aeneas and Lavinia in its assessment of new directions.
Putnam's article brings Cacus, Pallas and Dido into fresh focus as he interprets the
final scene of the epic. The conclusion is a demonstration of the epic's force: "the
last lines of the poem look not to any new-found sense of order which might have
been reached from an ethical use of arms leading to reestablishment of order but to
the life of Aeneas' final victim suffering the ultimate indignity of death." (19).
Charles Segal's article offers a steady, sensible discussion of the elements of sound
criticism of the classics. Suerbaum deals with the motif of divine guidance for
Aeneas in the pre-Vergilian and Vergilian tradition. Taliercio studies Callimachean
aetiology and Vergilian practice in the accounts of Alba Longa, Misenum, Palinurus,
and Caieta. Thomas (94) offers bright new insights into the persons and experiences
involved in Aeneas' quest and recalls Brooks Otis' dictum ( Virgil : A Study in Civilized Poetry , [Oxford 1963] 385) that Aeneas as hero "overcomes Dido and Turnus
inside himself." Vicenzi's review of Carthage and its recurrences in the epic
highlights fides Punica. Deryck Williams' offering prefaces each book with a survey
of subject matter and treatment, and with characteristic care and insight, provides a
general discussion of the epic's literary qualities in an Introduction. A glossary, and
four appendixes (all models of fuller literary appreciation) complete this memorable
guide for the general reader.
