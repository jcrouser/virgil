51. Dorothea Wender, 'From Flesiod to Homer by way of Rome,' Virgil's
Ascraean Song 59-64.
Betensky examines the
imagery of war in the Georgics where
warfare
against nature is both a metaphorical ideal and an intrusive reality. Her final
remarks concern the farmer's life as the lone continuity, threatened yet surviving, but always on the brink between Golden Age and disaster. Boyle is
concerned
with
the poem's politico-historical frame. Ambivalence, veering
towards pessimism, is pervasive and tragic. Human impotence, vulnerability,
dissolution and failure; brutishness, pitilessness, injustice (or amorality) are
endemic aspects of the mortal world. There are darker tones to the Laudes
Ita/iae. Boyle's essay, contained inthe Ramus Collection which he has edited,
is a 'bravura' exercise aimed at discovering the nature and meaning of the
enigmatic Georgics. Peter Connor argues that Vergil uses description to work
on the reader's imagination with the goal of creating a knowledge which is
not mere knowing but fresh attitudes. Vergil's most mature examination of
otium, for P. J. Davis,
is to be found in the Georgics. Patricia Johnston's
study of the agricultural golden age, inter alia, shows how the bees of G. 4
become
the realization of the nova progenies of E. 4. Vergil's deliberate
contrast between Aristaeus and Orpheus marks the displacement of the pastoral by the agricultural ideal. The healing role of Apollo assists the passage to
this more complex stage of civilization. Gretchen Kromer discusses the didactic convention and the assumptions underlying its use exemplified for Vergil
by
Lucretius' De Rerum Natura.
She studies the didactic language
in the
Georgics with emphasis on the poet's narrative or rhetorical stance and argues
that Vergil used the convention to examine and question the basic assumptions of the genre. Gary Miles views the Georgics as a case-study of efforts by
contemporary Romans to respond to chaotic late Republican times, when
idealizations of country life and 'reactionary' attempts to recover the agrarian
past were abroad. Miles' perceptive study
treats the poem as a series of
meditations on several visions of agrarian life. Frances Muecke's study of G. 2
is an insightful study of the self-conscious aspects of Vergil's didactic poem.
She provides detailed analyses of such passages in the Georgics. The poem
finds its moral and poetic justification
in transforming Lucretius' search for
individual happiness into a search for the well-being of society. O'Loughlin
sees the Georgics as more than a hymn to the work ethic. Vergil's poem is a
celebration of labor as a necessary concomitant of festive otium. Dorothea
Wender's essay relates the Georgics to its literary predecessors and provides a
list (p. 60) of literary borrowings or reminiscences inthe Georgics as basis for
her brief discussion. The progress is from Hesiodic tradition to Epicureanism
(G. 1, 2), thence to the tough-minded impersonal view of Homer ( G. 3, 4).
