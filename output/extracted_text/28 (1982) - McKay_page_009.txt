De Grummond examines Vergil's feelings at the close of the epic in light
of Juno's feelings at the outset. Juno's enduring, destructive wrath and resentment, born of hatred, and self-love, initiate the poem's movement. Aeneas'
quick, accelerating wrath, and resentment, born of his love for others, terminates the action; his acceptance of necessity leads to the resolution, and to
general prosperity. Kinsey interprets Troianus. . . Caesar (1.286) as implying
Augustus; and argues that Sidon was the dominant partner (with Tyre) for
Vergil. Segal's examination of lopas' song responds to Kinsey (Emerita 47,
1979, 77-86) and argues that 'symbolic' meaning should not be conceived too
narrowly; neither literalism, nor allegory, is a valid approach to lopas' song.
