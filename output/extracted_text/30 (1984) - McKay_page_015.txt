Alfonsi discovers likely instances of Vergilian borrowings from Calvus and
some exceptional uses. Caesar's youthful work, Oedipus, may provide some
sidelights on Vergil's 'tragic' repertoire. Bertram distinguishes Homer's windkeeper, Aeolus {Odyssey lO.lff.), from Vergil's version (Aeneid 1.52ff.). Vergil makes radical departures in his depiction of the character whose attitudes
and needs frequently prefigure those of Aeneas. Halperin's book and article
provide useful background for a reading of Vergil's Eclogues. The article on
Daphnis's forebears aligns Idyll 1 with ancient Near Eastern literature and
religious contexts. The 'religious aura' clinging to Daphnis enhanced his role
as presiding genius of the Sicilian landscape. Narducci associates Cicero, Rep.
6.10, and De Senectute 46
with Vergil, Aeneid 1.748; 6.160, and 8.309.
Neubourg regards increpuit densis alis (Georgics 1.382) as a faithful translation of Aratus' original thereby indicating that Vergil's forecasting device
was not original but owed its inspiration to the Greek model. The correspondence with Norden is yet another tribute to Wilamowitz's integrity and insight
