Bellei offers a new interpretation of the Moretum as erotic satire, turning
to recent Black feminist scholarship on pornography to argue that the
presence of sex does not automatically negate the agency of Scybale,
the African woman described in the poem as Simulus’s custos. Carmeli
suggests that at Catullus 13.8, plenus sacculus … aranearum is probably
to be understood in a metaliterary sense, as evidenced by the reuse of
the same metaphor in the Culex. In the light of literary evidence from the
first centuries BCE and CE, Haley contends that it is plausible that the
Romans were aware of skin-color differences and that these played a role,
among other factors, in the social construction of difference. The Romans
in Augustus’s day were more keenly aware of different cultural practices
(especially those of African societies) than we have given them credit for,
as the Aeneid and the Moretum illustrate. Hardy questions whether the
adjective densas at Culex 108 qualifies pecudes or umbras and evaluates the
options. Kayachev provides an introduction, Latin text, translation into
English, and commentary to Lydia. Olszaniec (2023b) offers textual notes
on Ciris 534–535.
