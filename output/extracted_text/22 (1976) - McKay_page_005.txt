37. M. C. J,Putnam, 'Italian Virgil and the Idea of Rome,9 Janus: Essays
in Ancient and Modern Studies (ed. Louis L. Orlin), (Ann Arbor, Mich.,
1975)
171-99,
with
Response
by Charles Wittke, 201-3, and Discussion,
203-10.
Putnam's scrutiny of Georgics 2, 136-76 (Laudes Ita/iae) and the end of
Georgias 2 (with pertinent epic passages) detects ambiguities inV's feelings
about Rome as a political entity. Human nature's imperfections trammel and
embarrass Saturnia teiius and the peoples of Italy. The same tension between
order and disorder, evident in the symbolism of violence, suppressed or rampant, pervades the Aeneid. Putnam finds Vergil repeatedly preoccupied with
thè reality of power's misuse,
the innocent Eden invaded and even militarized. Putnam's article challenges the 'idealistic' eulogy with disturbing ambiguities which, in his view, surface repeatedly in the 'natural' and 'human'
setting.
