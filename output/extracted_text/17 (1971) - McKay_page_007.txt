The problem of authorship continues to bedevil Vergilian scholarship. Barrett's
essay (59) examines past arguments and conclusions relative to the authorship of the
Culex from several approaches, aesthetic, statistical, and comparative, and argues in
favour of the historical approach. Since internal criteria have yielded negative results,
scholars should respect the judgment of poets of the first century and share their credulity. Elsewhere (60) Barrett considers the relationship of Lucretius 2,14-61 and Georg,
2.458-540 to Culex 58-97. He argues that the Culex was probably written between De
Rerum Natura and the Georgics and so supports the belief that Culex was a youthful
work of Vergil. Lyne (62) favours a post-Vergilian date for Ciris, and regards it as a
product of the second century which entered the Vergilian corpus by accident.
Knecht's volume (61) commands respect for its sanity and reserve. One is left with a
conviction that Ciris was a second century epyIlion consonant with the Vergilian
centones and the concept of the Vergilianus poeta. Renehan (63) prefers Idae at
Culex, 163. Skutsch (64) studies Ciris 135ff., and Moretum 39ff. Stegen (65) regards
Culex 272-6 as imitative of Vergil's style and technique. Van den abeele (66) detects a
bucolic atmosphere in the Dirae and an elegiac theme in the Lydia.
