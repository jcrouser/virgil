29. John Van Sickle, The Design of Virgii's Bucoiics. (Roma 1978).
Hardie's review suggests that it is a useful exercise to rethink E. 8, 6-13
(with Bowersock) as an address to Octavian, 'though Ajax's sponge sticks in
the throat (276). Van Sickle's study provides a fresh synthesis of numerical
pattern, style, genre, theme, and character and argues that an evolutionary
poetics is the basis of design. 'The plan and the theoretical import of Virgil's
book were perceived as something new and a challenge inpoetics.' (10). Van
Sickle approaches his ideal of "well-tempered criticism" and his arguments
for 'a highly artful and unified whole' require critical and respectful attention
by scholars.
