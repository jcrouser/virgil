Closa detects Celtic elements in Vergil's description of nature, his evocation
of celtic peoples (cf. A. 8.655-62) and in vocabulary. For Duhigg, smoothness
equates with the absence of interverbal consonantal clashes ( A . 1.1; 2.1; 10.1; 11.1;
12.1). The effect is one of greater euphony. Its appearance in opening lines seems
the result of chance rather than deliberate design. Garrison's beginner's Latin
textbook is designed for three weeks and provides A. 1 lines 1-207 as basis for a
taste of unedited Latin. Horsfall studies the impact on Vergil of the manner and
language of inscriptions known to him (A. 7. Iff.; 11.57f.; B. 4.7; A. 11.43, and
12.648). Horsfall's samples are arguably epigraphic in origin. Reinke offers a representative selection of alliterative lines in A. 1-6. Salat studies instances of the use of
ingens in the Georgics and Aeneid , and finds an analogue in the use of immanis and
immensus.
