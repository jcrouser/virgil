Codoñer comments on Aen. 1.81-123. Phillips suggests that Dido's behaviour at Aen. 1.561 does not reflect embarrassment or undue modesty; her
attitude is physically and psychologically apt. Borzsák detects oriental influence in the prodigy at Aen. 2.682ff. Cressey argues that Aen. 3.714 should be
assigned to Anchises' episode and not to Aeneas' last agony (pace R. D.
Williams, ad. loc.). Nenci stresses that humilemque (Aen.
3.522) denotes
'lowlying,' 'barely visible above the water surface." Quint underscores the
struggle between present and past in Aeneid 3 and examines episodes where
present actions are guided by memories of the dead past and where the theme
of forgetting appears with political implications for the new order of Augustus. Roti's view is that Aeneas stands 'alone' as sole leader after Anchises. Zijl,
Rix,
Pauw and Zeitsman unite in their comments on Aen. 3.568-87, and
655-81. Ameye analyses Aen. 4.298-9 (fama, eadem impia) and rejects the
notion of Aeneas' character as impius.
Bustamante treats Aen. 4.554-72.
Hardie accepts deliberate ambiguity in Aen. 4.48 If (and Aen. 6.796f) with a
conflation of images (mythological and scientific) of Atlas. Capponi treats
Aen.
5.213-217.
Rose examines
the
simile
at Aen.
5.270-81
as a foreshadowing of the loss of ships, separation from comrades, and safe arrivals in
Italy. Barigazzi studies the demons (Aen. 6.273-81) in conjunction with Cicero, De Nat. Deor, 3.17.44 (18.47) and Lucretius DRN 3.59-81. Kinsey
responds to D. P. Fowler (LCM 7: 5 [May 1982] 76) and excites Fowler's
renewed attack on Aen. 6.392-4; Kinsey's parting shot (?) responds to Fowler
again. The editor, J. Pinsent, grapples with the Gordian knot and suggests:
'You are armed and alive, so I cannot take you. I got no joy of previous cases
(also armed) and they were of divine descent' (as you are not, since Aeneas'
divine descent is unmentioned until 403-4). Pinsent prefers Fowler over Kinsey in the latest exchange. Wlosok studies Aeneid 6 with attention to the
function of Anchises' speech (724ff). Basto argues that Aen. 7.10 should be
seen in a symbolical and literal way; symbolically raduntur implies that the
Odyssey-half of the epic has ended; the adventures of Odysseus-Aeneas are
past and another invocation is required (Aen. 7.44-5). Blänsdorf introduces
Apollonius Rhodius, Argon.
