Gillis's study of Eros and Death is a bracing interpretation of the epic along
Freudian and Jungian lines, with some address also to Hindu sources. The
undertones, ambiguities and latent excitement of Vergil's poetry are a constant concern in Gillis's reflections on the marriage at Carthage, Pallas and
Aeneas, Aeneas and Turnus, and Eros and Politics. But the arguments and
interpretations

important points in the epic: the storm (Aeneid l);the Gauls on the Capitol,
and Actium, on Aeneas' Shield (Aeneid 8). Mills applies Eliade 's notion of
sacred space as an interpretive tool for understanding the epic. According to
Mills, the concept is central to the poem's thematic movement and meaning.
Monti's study of the Dido Episode wins praise from Putnam for its originality
and fresh departures. Nisbet likens the epic to a great novel of war and
politics. The imperator finally wins his spolia opima from the defeated Turnus, but his supreme command has eluded him. Seneca's injunction - imperare sibi maximum impérium est (Epist. 113.30) -
