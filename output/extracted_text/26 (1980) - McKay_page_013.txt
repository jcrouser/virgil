The shadowy figure of Cornelius Gallus has come into sharper focus lately.
The fragmentary elegiacs discovered at Qasr Ibrîm have been submitted to
searching analysis by several scholars (131) and Vergil's links with the careeristpoet have claimed some attention (op. cit., 148-55). Judge's article introduces
the inscription at Philae (ILS 8995),
the Vatican obelisk, and the
Cleveland
portrait (?)
(cf. G. Grimm, JDA! 85 [1970]
158-70). Dorothy
Thompson's search
for an historical background for the Tazza Farnese
in
Naples leads to Actium. The spirit and detail of Vergil's description are similar;
there
is no place for rejoicing, only lamentation (cf. A. 8.707ff., and
680). Thompson suggests Gallan (and Octavian) associations and speculates
that Vergil may have seen a comparable tableau in the triumphal procession.
Lee suggests that the fragmentary elegiacs may be seen as parts of one complex poem rather than as separate epigrams, and Van Sickle compares the P.
Qa$r
Ibrîm lines with Callimachus Epigram 15(Pf.) where he detects comparable arrangement of words and other parallels. Boyancé argues that Horace
Epode 16 preceded Vergil Ed. 4. Saylor suggests that the storm of Horace c.
1, 2 may have found inspiration in the storm of Aeneid 1 (or Geòrgie 1).
Ferguson detects word play, imitation of Catullus and Vergil, golden lines,
rhyme, rhythms, and effects reminiscent of Vergil in Ovid's Metamorphoses.
Griffin argues
that Ovid's Metamorphoses, though
less well organised than
Vergil's epic, offers an equally valid interpretation of reality. Putnam argues
that
Propertius
measured
his thought in 3.22 against Geòrgie 2
(Laudes
Italiae) and that the moral dilemmas of the Aeneid may have been equally
important to Ovid. Rutledge finds similarities between Fasti 5.637-62, and
Aeneid 8.31-67.
