Basson analyses the speeches of Jupiter (Aen. 1.257-96) and Anchises
(Aen. 6.752-892) along with the shield description (Aen. 8.626-731) to determine Vergil's view of Roman history. Effe examines VergiPs narrative technique and stresses emotional subjectivity in the epic. Lyne dismisses 'failure'
on the part of Aeneas or Vergil. Vergil never obscures the reality of war;
imperialistic
aggression
involves violence and
imperfect
motivations may
direct even the greatest hero. Phillips studies landscape in the epic and the
Georgics and traces progression in its poetic use from the Eclogues. Pöschl's
address underscores the humanism of Vergil's poetry. Scanlan provides topics
and questions based primarily on Aeneid 1-6 for secondary school teachers.
The questions are designed to excite discussion and serve as essay topics and
material for test papers.
Williams sheds new light on the deeper sense of classical poetry by providing an analysis of Vergil's literary technique; his search for meaning offers
new insights for thoughtful readers.
