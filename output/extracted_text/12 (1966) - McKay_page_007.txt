The
patterns
of word order and the distribution of dactyls
and
spondees in the hexameter line have become
increasingly
important
factors in the study of structure in the works
of Vergil, particularly in
the Aeneid.
Conrad (34) and Pearce (35) follow comparable lines of
research.
Conrad's survey is more historical.
He shows that there
was a tradition of wordorder in Latin hexameter poetry going back to
Homer.
He also shows how each of the patterns
of separation of substantive and attribute is formed, and what effects it makes.
Vergil,
among the Roman poets, proved most loyal to Homer; like Horace he
was ready to appropriate a tradition, to master the inherited forms and
express his own national and personal character through them.
Duckworth's researches (35), amplified in this volume
of Vergilius, deal
with metrical procedures, with the frequencies of sixteen metrical patterns, and with the frequencies
of repeated, opposite, and reverse patterns in adjacent lines.
His major finds are e.g., the Catullan nature
of Eclogue 4, and the surprising manner in which Aeneid 10-12 differ
metrically from the rest of the epic.

poetry suggest
