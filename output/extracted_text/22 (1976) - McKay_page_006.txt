Blümel's classroom exercise provides excellent guidance and a valuable
bibliography. Sanderlin shows how Vergil balances Aeneas' weaknesses (dependency,
homesickness, and doubt)
with pietas in Aeneid 3.
Rudd's
brilliant
study
attributes Didoís disaster not tò guilt but to
her love of
Aeneas. 'Whatever her deserts may have been (and I have argued that itis not
easy to make a clear Case against her) she did not deserve to suffer so cruelly.'
The idea of divine sport recurs: As flies to wanton boys are we to the
gods;/They kill us for their sport. Strain detects moral implications in luxu
(Aen. 4. 193) and alters Austin's version oí facta atque infecta (Aen. 4, 190)
to something approximating the Christian formula, 'sins of commission and
omission'. Pavlovskis appraises the role of the old and the young inAeneid 5
as an embodiment of the idea of the continuity of generations and as token
of the historical process within which Aeneas (and the poet) finds himself.
Bahr-Volk underscores the mood of ambiguity, uncertainty, and vagueness in
the Sixth Aeneid. But the cumulative effect is meaningful: 'ambiguities clarifyother ambiguities in a subtle paradox leading to a true view of reality.'
Bahr-Volk suggests that Aeneas and Deiphobe leave by the ivory gate 'not
because they are falsa insomnia but because they are like falsa insomnia; they
must return to the Upper World to wander through a warped reality, unclarified by the Underworld and
its manes.* The Austin Panel Discussion deals
with the final scene of the epic.
