Wilkinson's study of The Georgics of Virgil (Cambridge, 1969)
has aroused dormant interest for Vergil's capo lavoro, two thousand
years old. Escanciano's version and notes are often obvious, sometimes
perceptive (19, 20). Borzsák (22) supplies new insights on the Libyan
and Scythian digressions in G. 3. His comparative study of arguments
and techniques in Poseidonius are important and profitable. Capponi (23)
struggles with the unidentifiable acalanthis which may perhaps be linked,
by association, with alcyon (cf. G. 2, 328). Coleiro's article (25) explores
the double allegory underlying the community of bees and the Aristaeus
episode in G. 4, and raises the ghost of C. Cornelius Gallus again. For
Colleiro there are positive links between Aristaeus and the elegist, for
both share guilt, against Eurydice or Augustus, both suffer loss, bees
and political amicitia, and the shepherd's pardon was pattern for Gallus
had he been contrite to Augustus and appealed for mercy. Colleiro argues,
somewhat desperately, that Aeneid 1,2, and 4 inspired comparable passages and expressions in G. 4, thereby implying that the Aristaeus episode
was (as Servius remarked, and Büchner and Richter have accepted) inserted after the firstpublication of the Georgics in 29; Gallus' suicide in
26 ВС therefore induced Vergil to issue a second version of the fourth
Geòrgie. Grimm's article (26) provides fresh evidence for Gallus' role
in the 30's. Wormell (27), with characteristic clarity, finds an artistic
role for the bees in the composition of the Georgics. In their tiny, not
wholly human way, they bridge the gap between the agricultural tragedy
of Book 3 and the human tragedy of Book 4. Fischer's dissertation (29)
offers important comment on the geographical digressions in the Georgics
