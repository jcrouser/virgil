the Aeneid, and of primary themes {furor, fides,and regnum) in Propertius 4,
7, and 8. Ball's survey of Tibullus contains material relevant to Vergil. Basto's
study of Horace, Odes 1.3 emphasizes the mock-heroic, Vergilian traits of the
propempticon. The parallels are evident largely in Aeneid 1-7. If sailing is
accepted
as a metaphor
for poetic
composition,
Horace
shares
Vergil's
audacia in poetry (with Prometheus, Daedalus, and Hercules). Collectively the
elements combine to suggest that there was some mutual influence between
the poets. Moles argues that Aen. 2.557-8 was influenced by Asinius Pollio's
account of and reflections on the death of Pompey the Great. Nicoli finds
recollections of the Aeneid structure in Ovid's account of Cupid, Apollo, and
Daphne.

Gallus
papyrus
recovered from Qasr Ibrim.
Romano detects further evidence of the encounter of Octavian with Vergil at
Atella in Horace, Odes 3.4.37-40. Vergil and Horace together read the Georgies to Octavian at Atella in 29 BC. Traill adopts a new approach to the
question of the relevance of Vergil to the second half of Horace, Odes 1.3.
Horace progresses from the condemnation of the first mariner, through the
gigantomachy, to the contemporary political scene. Vergil's departure for
Greece, judging by the closing stanzas of the ode, may have been politically
motivated. The poet may have been an agent for Augustus or Maecenas
. . . West re-examines the Gallan fragment from Qasr Ibrim.
