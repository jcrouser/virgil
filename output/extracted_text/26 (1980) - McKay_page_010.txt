between

Palinurus and Caieta accounts.
Berényi-Révész finds dementia wanting in Vergil's hero. Fides and amicitia
are the bonds of association between Aeneas and his companions. Their harmonious links (marked by the use of iaeti) characterise the later unanimity
between princeps, senate, and people. Elftmann notes the division between
youth and old age in the epic; Vergil's interest
in the lifetime and age of
individuals
is
remarkable.
Ross and
Trahmann
are both concerned
with
Ascanius who emerges as a pattern and ideal, a standard by which others may
measure and direct their conduct. Thill argues that the Roman national virtues of virtus, fides, aná pietas were present inHomer's Hector. Van den Berg
examines the character of Aeneas inhis speech (A. 4.333-61). West treats the
tragic careers of Anna and Juturna and finds the latter's tragedy more poignant. Pöschl's suggestion that the Aeneid is an epic of love where the deepest
tragedy
is that people 'loved too much' should properly include Anna and
Juturna whose careers are bound to one another through verbal parallels and
plot similarities.
