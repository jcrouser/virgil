After his previous review of Vergil's Epicureanism and of
Epicurean motifs in his poetry, Castelli (1) proceeds to a study of
hedone (otium) in the Eclogues , and of Epicurean expression and
and concept in the death and apotheosis of Daphnis {Eel. 5).
Notopoulos (2) has discovered that there is fifth century evidence
for scientific knowledge among the satyrs ( Oxy . Pap . 8 (1911)
1083, p. 60) and that Silenus' scientific expertise in Eel . 6 is not
a Vergilian novelty. Des Abbayes' verse translation of the Eclogues
is perfectly attuned to the original (3). Hornsby's 'new method'
text (4) provides, inter alia , criticism, questions and exercises
for Eclogues 1, 4, and 5. Berg (5) finds Prometheus as the forerunner of Daphnis in E. 5 . He suggests an Aeschylean inspiration
for the Eclogue , a satyr-play and the more serious tradition which
Aeschylus advances in the P. V. Daphnis, as devoted benefactor
of the pastoral world, finds his benefactor-model in the philanthropic Prometheus. Julius Caesar and his successor could also
be imagined in the same heroic dimension. Eleanor Leach (6)
assesses the second Eclogue as a paradigm for the entire collection, which favors the pattern of withdrawal and return in Corydon's
conduct. 'For him all pastoral is a deliberate experiment, and the
pastoral world is a temporary place of discovery and organization
for the poet and for man'.
