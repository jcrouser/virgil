Julian colony at Carthage as a Vergilian reference. Romoeuf analyses the
ekphrasis at Aeneid I, 466-93 as something less monumental than literary,
and as prelude to the llioupersis which ensues. Fleck and Kraggerud are both
concerned with the contested Helen episode inAeneid 2, 567-88. Exclusion
of the passage remains a personal prerogative of scholars and editors. Stegen
shows how Vergil prepares the Trojans to receive the horse into the city.
Phillips finds evocations of Juno Lucina (and Pronuba) in Vergil's goddess.

Dido Tragedy is sound and directional. Christmann and the Kopffs are both
concerned with the Gates of Sleep at the close of Aeneid 6. The latter ismore
instructive and original: Aeneas is not meant to be regarded as 'false' dream
of a deceitful Roman and Augustan glory. However 'unreal' the vision, Aeneas
returns as agent and messenger of the Manes with the mission to found Rome.

and

thereby did with the topography what he does with tradition.' (483). Pöschl
offers new insights into the complexity of the Daedalic temple doors. The
