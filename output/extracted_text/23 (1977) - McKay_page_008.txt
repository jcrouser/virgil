Bryce treats the nature of the relationship and the tragedy underlying the
liaison between Aeneas and Dido. The tragedy lies in the relationship 'between two basically incompatible people - a relationship which ...
is meaningless and
unproductive, and fraught with misunderstandings, suspicions,
fears, resentment, and despair.' (269). Burke's study of Amata's character
discloses her function as a scapegoat and accounts for her consistently improper behaviour and for her exceptional attachment to her would-be son-inlaw whose death is intimately associated with her own. Fleck (73) examines
Aeneid II, 567-88 with useful comments on the contested scene involving
Aeneas with Helen. Revesz suggests that Agrippa lies behind Achates. Achates
appears eleven times inAeneid I and only ten times elsewhere. Vergil's underscoring of amicitia and fides may reflect Augustus' concern.
