Ward Briggs offers a succinct, perceptive resumé of amor in G. 3, and the
Aeneid. West's contribution analyses the Vergilian plague in light of Lucretius1 horror in Athens. Lucretius shows more concern for accurate description
of observable phenomena, following Thucydides; Vergil, though indebted to
Lucretius, exhibits characteristic traits: rhetorical elaboration, emotion, anthropomorphism, religious asides, and moralizing. His tendency is to write for
effect and lose
sight of the
truth!
Betensky examines G. 4.149-227 and
studies aspects in which bees and Cyclopes reflect one another: birth, habitat,
autonomy, immortality, products, and control of violence. A series of anitheses summarizes the comparison of bees and Cyclopes: bees/Cyclopes =
above earth/ below earth; mobile/stationary; autonomous/dependent; mortal
/immortal; honey/thunderbolts;
special
farming/ special technology. 'The
logic of daily life, preoccupied with prescribing techniques and enumerating
species, gives way to the logic of mythology, which compares the essence of
things.' They inhabit different worlds but they can co-exist in a standoff. An
armistice exists between the incongrous worlds of farming and war as long as
forces which can destroy are directed towards creation, while Jupiter acts as
foreman on the celestial assembly line!
