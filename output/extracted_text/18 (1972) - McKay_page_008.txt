Deryck Williams brings insight and a measure of excitement to practically
every page of his textbook. He carefully notes his departures from the
text of Mynors and Hirtzel, and generally convinces one of the wisdom
of his choice. Students and lecturers will welcome this first volume with
joy and gratitude. The commentary (154-517) is refreshingly modern, nicely attuned to structure, thematic language and poetics. Austin (39), with
customary authority, style, and a measure of polemic, provides the first
detailed English commentary on Aeneid I since R.S. Conway's edition
(1935). The text and commentary are exemplary, and offer a fine inducement to studious, pleasurable reading. Segal (40) interprets the Iopas
vignette and the elaborate cosmological song in its immediate and larger
context. He notes that the subject matter enlarges to cosmic proportions
the imagery of fireand darkness which pervades the close of Aeneid
1. The bard's song telescopes present and future, individual and cosmic
destinies, into a single moment of poetic vision. The alignment of human
events with universal processes for mutual illumination is perfectly evidenced in Iopas' song where the broader, Platonic mysteries, are glimpsed
in this prelude to human tragedy. Ferguson (42) offers further comment
on the controlling images of fire and wound inAen. 4. Only the hearth-fire,
token of domesticity and family life, is missing. The wound has two
tangential aspects: (1) disease (fire and poison), and (2) the hunt (hunter
and quarry). Bertman's article (46) explores the polarity of generations
in Aen. 5, where the generation gap is given symbolic, forceful exposition.
Opperman (47) and Somville (48) provide critical studies of the Palinurus
episode in Aen. 5. Both concentrate on the Somnus passage, the implications of the intrusion and the response of the helmsman. Stégen (49)
compares and contrasts the Vergilian boxing match with Homer, Iliad,
23, 653-75, the obvious model, but with important structural differences.
Barbu (50) deals with the hierarchy of human values and the law of universal necessity discernible in Aen. 6. Dupont-Neraudau (52) combine to
give some novel insights into the Marcellus episode. Rieks (53) and Rutledge (54) are complementary exercises; Rieks is concerned with the
heroic pomp at the close, Rutledge with the Daedalic gallery at the outset
of Aen. 6 Rutledge argues that the artist is a creature of marked insight,
with interpretative gifts which can impart order, direction and definition
to men's lives; Aeneas studies the panels with a sense of their importance.
The labyrinth suggests the ordeal and the triumph which await Aeneas
in the underworld. Sabbadini's (5) Aeneid 6, with commentary, is a welcome reprint and worthy of wider study. Rev. R.V. Schoder's pictorial
study (56) of the Campi Phlegraei repeats his earlier article in Romanitas
9 (1970) 187-98. He provides superb photographs of the area which made
a profound impression on the poet and on modern Vergilians who follow
in his steps. Solmsen (58) argues against the tripartite scheme of Aen.
6, detected by Norden, Frances Norwood, and Otis, as irrelevant and
