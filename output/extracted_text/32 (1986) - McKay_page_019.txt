Carlsen's Bibliography lists works published between 1900 and 1983. Dal Santo's
study embraces Giovanni Pascoli's Nuovi Poemetti (1909). Fichter traces Vergilian
influence in Renaissance epic poetry, from 1450-1600. Fogelman studies the bucolic
genre, revitalized during the 20th century, and given a broader, often ironic, potential along with emotive intensity and breadth of awareness. Graves emphasizes that
Wordsworth had an acute understanding of the Aeneid and a remarkable ability to
preserve the nuances of Vergil's poetic style in his incomplete translation of the
epic. Huxley provides another sample of his ingenious verse. Kallendorf shows that
Bocaccio rejected Vergil's account of Dido in favor of the "historical" chaste Queen
of Carthage. Bocaccio saw his account as more accurate and certainly more acceptable from the standpoint of morality. Owen Lee's article offers vivid testimony to
the impact of Vergil's epic on Hector Berlioz, and on Les Troy ens. The echoes of
dactylic hexameters recur throughout the opera at decisive moments. The acrostich
devised by Dr. and Mrs. Robert Lloyd is challenging and entertaining, as well as
instructional. Low's Geòrgie Revolution examines English poetry between 1500 and
