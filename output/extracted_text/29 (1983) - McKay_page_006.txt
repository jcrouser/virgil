Roberts interprets Eclogue 1 as Vergil's redefinition of poetry as a mode
of mimetic, not quasi-historical action, and states the limits
of linguistic
power. Mayer studies Eclogue 2 to determine Corydon's status and argues
that servile status
is unacceptable by reason of his property holdings and
poetic decorum.
Fisher settles on Aratus, who symbolized astronomy, as
solution to the question posed in Eel. 3.40. Conon and Aratus are suitable
partners on Damoetas' cups. Courtney suggests that Vergil projects himself
from the marriage of Antony and Octavia to the future and imagines the
sequel, the birth of a son, in Eclogue 4. His reading of Bue. 4.60-61 is: 'begin,
little boy, to recognize your mother with a smile (a supernatural thing to do);
ten months (because you are a boy, not a girl) have brought long weariness to
your mother.' Pulbrook's allegorical interpretation favours Daphnis as Octavian. Ogawa finds a principle of unity in the labor motif, a factor derived from
Theocritus Idyll
7.
Geymonat
revives Savage's earlier thesis (1963) that
Thyrsis is a reflection of the youthful Horace in Eclogue 7. Tracy's article
coincides with Frederick Brenk's study of the sepulcrum Bianoris (Vergilian
Bibliography, 1981-82, #26).
