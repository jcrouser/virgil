Avilés traces Catullian influence on Vergil (structure, vocabulary, and
metrics). Bettini treats A. 6.703 and Homer, Od. 24. Iff. Landolfi explores coincidences and contrasts in Vergil and Lucretius. Marastoni marshals Vergil's precursors and contemporaries in agricultural writings. Sanvitale matches vocabulary usage
in Catullus 64 and Aen. 4. Thornton observes that Vergil compares Dido with Venus,
not Diana. Nausicaa (likened to Artemis) is a passing sexual arousal for Odysseus,
whereas Dido is the object of a torrid passion, more suitable to the realm of Venus.
Van Sickle's essay will be reviewed with the remainder of the volume next year.
Wright regards Eclogue 1 as preface to the book, with programmatic intentions: to
provide a distinctively Roman pastoral which derived from Theocritus and
Callimachean principles.
