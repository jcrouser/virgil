Arrian, the epic poet, translated the Georglcs into Greek hexameters; Constantine used a hexameter rendering of Eclogue 4. Baldwin's article
adduces the evidence of extant papyrus texts to indicate that the poet's fame
persisted in allusions into the mid-1 5th century. Horsfall notes a thematic
reworking of Aeneid 1.34ff in Apuleius. Newton's article treats the catabasis
motif in Satyricon 72 and suggests that Petronius intended the scene in
Trimalchio's bath to recall Vergil's vision of the Elysian Fields. The comic
inversion and incongruity are assessed: Encolpius, as an anti-Aeneas, Trimalchio as an unlikely Orpheus, and the guests, as heroes who fell at Troy.
ANCIENT LIVES
184. Lawrence Lipking,
The Life of the Poet: Beginning and Ending
Poetic Careers (Chicago 1982).
185. H. Naumann, 'Suetonius' Life of Virgil: The Present State of the
Question,' HSCP 85 (1981) 185-7.
186. H. Naumann, 'Was wissen wir von Vergils Leben?,' AU 24:5 (1981)
5-16.
Naumann, in both English and German, argues that the Vergilian Vita of
Donatus' commentary is the Life composed by Suetonius, and adduces facts
and other considerations to support the verdict. Lipking's venturesome but
uneven study is concerned with the life and personality of poets, including
Vergil,
with
the biography that invades
their poems.
But there are some
disturbing arguments: 'the death of Misenus stands for his city's cursed past
which must be buried before the survivors can be reborn;' and Turnus is
characterized as a 'stone-age savage' in a strained interpretation of Aeneid
12.896-8.
