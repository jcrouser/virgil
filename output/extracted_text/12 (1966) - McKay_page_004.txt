The Vergilian

in Renaissance and

Durer,
Breughel, Elsheimer, Claude and Poussin, Corot and Blake, all share
elements
of the Vergilian landscape, the myth of ideal rusticity.
Sir
Kenneth Clark has defined the Ideal or Vergilian landscape as 'essentially an evocation of the antique world, with all its fullness of life and
its confidence in the body, but also with some qualities which the ancient world lacked, some half-lights, some backward glances.'
(Landscape into Art.
London, 1949.
p. 55).
The Gallus inscription found
recently on the Vatican obelisk in St. Peter's Square continues to excite
discussion.
(14).
Gilbert Highet has challenged the earlier interpretations which located the obelisk in Egypt (Heliopolis) when Gallus added
the inscription.
Gallus' title, PRAEFECTUS FABRUM, in conjunction
with the phrase, FORUM JULIUM FECIT, might best attach to Gallus'
role as architect of the Forum Julium in Rome, completed by Augustus
(cf. Mon. Ane. 20, 12-4).
FECIT, rather than the normal PERFECIT,
may exemplify the vanity which finally brought Gallus to ruin and suicide.
It is also highly unlikely, in Highet' s view, that the colony
of
Forum Julium (mod. Fréjus) is implied, or that Gallus, as Governor of
Egypt, would refer to himself, in an Egyptian context, merely as Praefectus Fabrum, particularly when he felt emboldened to call himself
elsewhere, in the Philae inscription (ILS 8995) PRAEFECTUS ALEXANDREAE ET AEGYPTII PRIMUS.
Gallus' unquiet ghost keeps coming back to excite the archaeologist as
it once excited Vergil and the
Augustan elegists.
