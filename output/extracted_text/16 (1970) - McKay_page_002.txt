The present survey of Vergilian scholarship is the eighth in the Vergilius series.
Previous reviews may be found in Numbers 9 (1963) 33-6; 10 (1964) 40-44; 11 (1965)
40-47; 12 (1966) 39-45; 13 (1967) 35-41; 14 ( 1968) 16-27; and 15 (1969) 42-52.

Two remarkable and partly complementary studies of Vergilian pastoral have
appeared, both products of extraordinary critical sensitivity and encompassing diverse
aspects of the poetic art. Rosenmeyer's study (9) ranges over the larger pastoral
terrain, but with brilliant insights into the Vergilian form; Putnam's study (8) considers
each Eclogue as a separate artistic work and concentrates on structure and vocabulary. Putnam
redefines the poems as profoundly ethical, as vehicles for ideas and
social comment. Schindel (2) detects Ulixes redux behind Meliboeus; Nethercut (4)
and Veremans (5) are concerned with symbolism as guide to poetic unity in the
