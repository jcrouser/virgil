where Vergil emerges as Magister Morum. McKay examines Vergilian traits in
paintings by Claude Lorrain, Nicolas Poussin, Peter Brueghel, and A. J.M.
Turner. Martin records his impression of a poetic palimpsest of the Aeneid
composed by Denis Guenon. The contemporary version, which introduces
analogies with the Boat People, draws on the first six books to yield three
distinct episodes. The modern poem is a free adaptation with a powerful
impact. Mueller is concerned with the interaction of epic and dramatic traditions in Racine's use of the Dido tragedy as a model for Bérénice (230-248).
Michael Murrin's study contains fourteen pages on Vergil. Nascimento's article treats Nicholas Trevet, an English Dominican at Oxford during the 13th
and 14th centuries, who composed the earliest known commentary on Livy.
His commentary on the Bucolics survives in two 15th century North Italian
mss., one in the Laurentian Library, Florence, the other in Evora (Portugal).
Pope's doctoral thesis argues that Spenser's use of national history in his
heroic poem was directly modelled on Vergil's use of historical material in the
Aeneid.
She supplies new interpretations of the function of prophecies in
Aeneid 1-6 and explores Aeneas' reasons for his liaison with Dido. Rudat is
concerned with fatum, pietas, sapientia, and labor in the Aeneid as reflected
in the great themes of Chaucer's work. Schuchard deals with Nahum Tate's
Dido and Aeneas (1689) and includes illustrations by Franz Cleyn. Sinclair is
concerned with the Aeneid as sacrum poema in Macrobius Sat. 1.24.13. The
temple metaphor at
Sat. 1.24.12 leads to the conclusion that the sacrum
poema (= Aeneid) is not to be regarded as pagan counterpart to the Bible.
Siro treats an anonymous work in the Laurentian Library, Florence, drawn
from late antique or early mediaeval times. Goldsmith's doctoral thesis is
concerned with a prose translation of the Aeneid by Joost van den Vondel,
enhanced with 140 pen and ink drawings by Leonaert Bramer. Thill's article
traces Vergilian elements in Jacob Balde's 17th century Latin and German
odes
(Carmina Lyrica). Wofford's doctoral thesis argues that Vergil's technique dramatizes the limitations and bleakness of the world wherein his characters act.
