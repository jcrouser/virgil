Ball publishes a 1949 letter from the American writer and literary critic
Edmund Wilson (1895–1972) to Gilbert Highet (1906–1978), which
ushered in reviews of Highet’s book The Classical Tradition and played
a part in Highet’s and Wilson’s admiration of Vergil. Bellei uses the lens
of absence to explore the use of Dido as a model for the two protagonists
of the Neapolitan Novels by the pseudonymous Italian novelist Elena
Ferrante (1943–). Mac Góráin’s interactive interpretation of the Aeneid
and the 1981 play Translations by the Irish dramatist and short-story
writer Brian Patrick Friel (1929–2015) helps us to look more closely at
language difference in the Aeneid. By puncturing the poem’s linguistic
uniformity with occasional references to language difference, Mac Góráin
argues, Vergil makes a remarkable statement of imperial whitewash and
supremacy. Vallortigara discusses the varying receptions of the myth of
Aeneas in the twentieth century and today.
TWENTY-FIRST CENTURY
Hinds, Stephen. 2025. “Bann Valley Eclogues.” In Augoustakis, Frangoulidis,
and Thorsen, 397–412.
Maurice, Lisa. 2018. “No Arms and the Man? Virgil’s Aeneid in Modern
Popular Culture.” SCI 37: 111–27.
Hinds offers a selective exploration of three “eclogues” by Seamus Heaney
in his 2001 collection Electric Light, which variously involve translation
of Vergil, adaptation of Vergil, and conversation with Vergil. Maurice
observes that the Aeneid, perhaps more than any other text, has had a
profound impact on Western literature and society. Yet, even though
Homeric receptions of the Trojan War continue to multiply, there have
been remarkably few receptions of the Aeneid in modern popular culture.
She offers an overview of its reception in popular fiction, children’s
literature, poetry, screen, and stage, before making suggestions about why
Vergil’s poem has failed to take hold in the way that Homer’s have.
