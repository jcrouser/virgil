(179-211) addresses the biographical notices attributed to M. Valerius Probus,
the first century grammarian. He provides a new text of Probus' account of
Vergil's life with divergences from Egnazio's 1507 Venetian edition (which
located Vergil's birthplace three miles from Mantua). Naumann deals with
fact and fiction in the Vergilian Lives.. Sanz Ramos is concerned with Vergilian Parthenius, the traditional scripsit Aeneidem . . . sed non emendavit; and
admonente Pollione scripsit Bucolicam. Vidal offers a critical analysis of the
Phocas
Life and provides a translation. Von Albrecht, with characteristic
clarity and suggestion, deals broadly with Vergil's life and work, his sources
and technique. Wünsche treats a probable portrait of Vergil in Munich's
Glyptotek.

the special qualities of Petrarch's manner of annotating the text as exemplified by several of his notes to Eclogue I. Petrarch's probing imagination,
spacious memory, and love of Rome are repeatedly evident in his comments
on works which were closely associated with his scholarly and poetic career.
