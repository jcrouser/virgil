VERGILIAN BIBLIOGRAPHY (1975-76)
The present survey of Vergilian scholarship is the fourteenth inthe VergiJius series. Classical
World 68.1
(1974), published
in association with the
Vergilian
Society,
Inc., contains
my
'Recent Work on Vergil:
A Bibliographical Survey, 1964-1973.' The 92-page Survey may be ordered from the
offices of THE CLASSICAL WORLD or from the Treasurer of the Vergilian
Society, Inc. Addenda and subsequent surveys have appeared in Vergilius 19
(1973), 20 (1974), and
21 (1975). The editor of 'Vergilian Bibliography'
welcomes reprints of articles and bibliographical notices for inclusion in the
annual review. The mailing address: Department of Classics, McMaster University, Hamilton, Ontario L8S 4M2, Canada.
BIBLIOGRAPHY
1. F. A. Peeters, Bibliography of VergiJ(Rome 1975). (Reprint of the
Bulletin of the American Classical League, New York, 1933).
2.
The
Vergiliana (Publius
Vergilius Maro). Microforms International
Marketing Co. (Pergamon Press, Inc.) (Elmsford, New York, 1975).
The Peeters bibliography requires expansion and caution regarding entries.
The
Vergiliana
consists
of
three
Vergil
collections
housed
at
Philipps
Academy, Andover, Mass: the Charles H. Forbes Virgil Collection, the Bancroft Collection of English Versions of Vergil's Poems, and the Ballard Collection of Translations. The collections are available either on 197
reels of
microfilm, or 5000 microfiche.
EDITIONS
3. N. Horsfall, rev., M. Geymonat, P.
Vergili Moronis Opera & R. D.
Williams, The Aeneid of Virgil, Books 7-12,JRS 54 (1974) 273-6.
4. D. A. West, rev., 'Two Editions of Virgil: Williams (7-12) and Geymonat', CR 26 (1976) 34-6.
APPENDIX VERGILIANA
5. Cipriano Conti, Catalepton XI: Lusus letterario o autentico epitaffio?
(Perugia 1975).
6. J. F.
Killeen, 'Tau gallicum (Vergil, Catalepton 2,4)', Orpheus 21
(1974) 57-9.
7. R.O. A.M. Lyne, 'Ciris 89-91,' CQ 25 (1975) 156-7.
8. D. O. Ross, Jr.,'The Culex and Moretum as Post-Augustan Literary
Parodies,' HSCP 79 (1975) 235-63.
Ross identifies Culex and Moretum as clever post-Vergil ian parodies. Culex
poses as a period piece but reflects contemporary (late Tiberian?) poetic
manners; Moretum is a superb parody wherein epic expressions and mannerisms are trivialized by their contexts or by the sense they convey. The
realistic touches are reflections of Alexandrian
and neoteric treatments of
the heroic indomestic settings.
