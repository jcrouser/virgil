Demaray discovers Milton's Paradise inVergil's Campania. Davie highlights
Vergilian
traits
in Thomas Hardy's poetry. Di Cesare provides a complete
annotated bibliography of one of Vergil's most famous followers. Evans (101)
treats Ovid's debt to Vergil's Scythian digression at Georgics 3, 349-83.
Guentner compares six secular polyphonic compositions which used part of
Dido's lament (Aen. 4, 642-60). Huxley's Latin verse compositions address
Nisus, with indebtedness to Anchises' faci/is iactura sepuIeri, and the Numidian larbas. Martyn treats a Welsh neo-Latinist (1564-1627). Mason's study
deals with the presence of Virgil and Dryden in Pope's translation of the Iliad
and detects the Vergilian voice in the poetic version. Schmidt argues that
Vergil may have provided bucolic inspiration for Thomas Mann's Death in
Venice.
