their verse translations of Vergil’s three major works. Tanfield’s threevolume companion to the Aeneid is designed for use by students reading
the work in English. The first volume offers a broad introduction to the
epic’s historical and philosophical background, to Vergil’s life and works
and his stylistic techniques, and to the epic’s reception, along with maps
and a general index. The following two volumes present a line-by-line
commentary on the poem that can be used with any edition of the Aeneid
in English or Latin. Les Quatre premiers livres des Eneydes du treselegant
poete Virgile, traduictz de Latin en prose Françoyse (1541) by the French
novelist Hélisenne de Crenne (1510–1552) translates only the first four
books of the Aeneid. In so doing, Weiner observes, Hélisenne produced a
radical intervention on Vergil’s epic. The Aeneid becomes not a celebration
of Roman nationalism but instead a protofeminist tale of tragic romance
and male perfidy. Weiner argues that there is something subversive in
adapting Greek and Latin literature to undermine Western imperialism,
racism, misogyny, and classism.
