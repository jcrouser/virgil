L.P. Wilkinson. The "Georgics" of Virgil: A Critical Survey. (Cambridge, 1969).
Wilkinson's long awaited study (16) has exceeded expectations. Wiljdnson
regards the poems as vividly descriptive with pleasure as the ultimate objective, not
didactic but entertainment. Wilkinson stresses the deep personal feeling and philosophical purpose, and detects the religious, political, and symbolic motifs which permeate the well wrought lines. His comprehensive study of the poem's Nachleben is
exemplary. His chapter on "Poetic Approach and Art" is concerned with rhythm and
diction, and provides a perceptive reading of the Aristaeus epyllion as dividend.
Wilkinson is antipathetic to symbolic interpretations and to numerological theories.
Martyn (13) treats Georgics I, 80-81 (parodied by Juvenal 3, 321-2) andren. 5,
353-8 in an entertaining fashion; Dorothea Wender (14) and Wankenne (15) offer
concise and convincing interpretations of the Aristaeus-Orpheus episode.
