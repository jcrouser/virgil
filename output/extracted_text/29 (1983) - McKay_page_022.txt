Abbolito's article treats Vergilian influences on Avitus' biblical epic. André
detects survivals of Vergil
in the works of Delacroix, Berlioz, and SainteBeuve, and explores Vergilian traits
in Italian romanticism. Baldwin comments on Procopius of Gaza's use of Aen. 2.573 (communis Erinys) to describe the burden of taxation in his Panegyricus in Imp. Anast. 14. Certainly
Vergil was a familiar author in early Byzantium. Cattareo salutes Gavin Douglas' innovative use of the heroic couplet in his Aeneid translation. Fichter
argues that Ariosto, Tasso and Spenser read Vergil with the eyes of St. Augustine. All translated the implicit melancholy and uncertainty of the Vergilian
vision into a sure knowledge that the triumph of their chosen dynasties was
an image of the Christian apocalypse. De Teves Costa studies Vergilian influence in painted ceramic tiles of the 16th-18th century in Portugal's mansions
and the University of Evora. Forte examines traces of Vergil's Aeneid in Luca
Signorelli, the Florentine Apollonio di Giovanni, Raphael, Mephaeus Vegius'
Thirteenth Book (1428), Ariosto's Orlando Furioso (1532), and Tasso's Gerusalemme Liberata (1581). Fuhrmann traces the influence of Vergil's Georgics
in the European tradition. Gransden treats W. H. Auden's jeu d'esprit, 'Secondary Epic' included in his Homage to Clio (1960). For Auden, Vergil's was 'a
Muse betrayed' by political consideration. Huxley offers a puzzle based on
five Vergilian verses. Kallendorf's doctoral thesis treats 14th and 15th century
criticism of Vergil, primarily Cristoforo Landino's Vergil Commentary (1488)
