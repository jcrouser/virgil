Hall's article (53) examines Scarron's burlesque of Vergil's
Aeneid in 1648-59. He regards Scarron's version as an elaboration
of the 4causerie en vers', a negative guide to the levels of the
sublime. The prelude offers a foretaste of the burlesque which
follows:
Je chante cet homme pieux
Qui vint charge de tous ses Dieux,
Et de Monsieur son pere Anchise,
Beau vieillard a la barbe grise.
Of Deiopeia, the nymph with whom Juno bribes Aeolus in A. i, 4un
vrai visage de poupée', Scarron writes:
Elle entend, et parle fort bied
L'espagnol and l'italien.
