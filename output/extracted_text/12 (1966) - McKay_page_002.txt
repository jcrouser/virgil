Segal (1) shows
greater concern with
the poetic
quality of the
poems than with the location of the farm, the tangled probably insoluble
problem of the confiscation and restoration
of the property.
His detailed analysis of Eclogues 1 and 9 as pendants to each other discovers,
inter alia, shadowy
elements
even
in Arcadia.
What
Panofsky has
called the 'vespertinal mixture of sadness and tranquillity' exists in
Vergil's repeated theme of exile from a peaceful, familiar world, and
constitutes Vergil's most personal contribution to poetry.
Fredericksmeyer (2) is not ready to accept Eclogue
1 as an outright eulogy of
Octavian.
He finds the poem ambivalent in its view of Octavian; his
past acts are assessed as productive of both good and evil, but he also
emerges as a saviour, a force for good, and a source of hope for the
future.
The familiar identification of Tityrus with Vergil, and the iuvenis
deus with Octavian, in Eclogue
1 is
challenged
by Grisart (3).
His
novel interpretation discovers a double objective: a masque involving
Vergil
and
the
youth Eros, combined with a dramatised
complaint
against expropriation of farms to compensate the veterans. Wilkinson
(4) examines Eclogues 1 and 9 in the light of pastoral conventions.
Allegorical interpretations are regularly embarrassed by the desire to
make all the details fit.
In fact Vergil's Eclogues are
Theocritean
pastorals with occasional outcroppings
of reality. Wilkinson's article,
though abbreviated, will serve as a corrective to H. J. Rose's literalmindedness in the criticism of the Bucolics, and as a useful corollary
to some of Vanderlinden's views (5).
Coleman's article (6) avoids the
biographical approach to the criticism of the Eclogues.
