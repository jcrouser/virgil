Rudd's chapter assesses various principles and arguable designs relating to
the architecture of the Eclogues. Each system contains a defect; none claims a
superiority which might suggest Vergil's handiwork. Rudd assumes that the
final pattern of the book was not a matter of primary importance to Vergil.
Rudd also introduces the first book of Horace's Satires for parallel discussion;
comparable questions arise. Scholars have been more concerned with the
texture than with the architecture of the collection. Rudd's criteria for the
poet's blueprint are cogent: the features of the plan should be large, bold, and
simple, certainly discernible when pointed out. 'The more subtle an architectural
theory is, and
the more it tries to explain, the less persuasive it
becomes.' Kollmann considers the function and place of Proper Names inthe
Bucolics where their appearance is exceptional. Kollmann assigns the names
to four groups according to the nature of their content: (1) Poetry, Song; (2)
Love; (3) Shepherds and their life; and (4) Idyllic world vs. Reality. Names
communicate associations and rouse the emotions; they activate information
and stimulate reactions. The Theocritean contribution of Proper Names is
relatively small. The articles by Boyle and Coleman are required reading for
students of the telogues. Schmidt's exercise with the chronological problems
attaching to the Bucolics is intricate and somewhat defeating; Rudd's chapter
is a useful companion piece.
