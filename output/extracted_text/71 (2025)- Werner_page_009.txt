Farrell notices that in the Aeneid, the Ethiopians are mentioned only
once (4.481), but the population returns in other contexts where they
are recognizable both intertextually—through the model of the Iliad, the
Odyssey, and some passages of Callimachus—and ethnographically. These
references make the presence of Ethiopians at the Carthaginian court
plausible. Marcolongo meditates on the lessons of rebuilding, recovery,
and renewal that can be learned from Vergil’s portrait of Aeneas as a model
of resilience. The Italian original has been translated into Portuguese (A
lição de Eneias, trans. Miguel Serras Pereira, Coimbra, 2020), Spanish (El
arte de resistir: Lo que la Eneida nos enseña sobre cómo superar una crisis,
trans. Juan Rabasseda Gascón and Teófilo de Lozoya, Barcelona, 2023) and
English (Starting from Scratch: The Life-Changing Lessons of Aeneas, trans.
Will Schutt, New York, 2022). Pearcy has noticed that on several occasions
in the Aeneid fate is imagined as a text. Divinities or divinely inspired
beings can read it, but human attempts to do so fail because our sequential
ordering of past, present, and future is inadequate for understanding
the folds and disruptions in linear time that knowledge of fate implies.
Suerbaum notes that in the Aeneid it is not so much winning as possessing
captured armor (spolia) that is problematic: in many cases, donning the
armor has disastrous consequences for both Trojan and Italian heroes.
