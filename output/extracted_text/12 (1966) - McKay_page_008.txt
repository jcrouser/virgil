The Perret
study (37) has been revised and eight new plates added.
The literary
criticism, particularly of the Eclogues, marks a sensitive, penetrating
incursion into the old hunting ground.
The Eclogues still constitute an
important gap in Vergilian scholarship, as does the postRenaissance
tradition
of Vergil.
Segal's
(38)
and Duckworth's
(39)
reviews
of
Brooks Otis' Virgil: A Study in Civilized Poetry are comprehensive
and instructive. Bruère's (40) review article of de Cesare's study of
the Christiad by Vida is masterly and enlightening. Copley's new verse
translation of the Aeneid (41) is a line-for-line version based on the
English iambic pentameter, but with free substitution
of anapaestic,
dactylic, and even trochaic feet for iambics.
Not words but ideas are
the translator's aim in a version that has aimed to preserve dignity,
clarity, and immediate comprehensibility in a compressed
style.
In
reaction probably
to the C. Day Lewis version, Copley claims
that
'every effort has been made to avoid the witty and mannered, the outré
and archaic, except where a truly ancient or fantastic idea seemed to
demand such expression.' Otis' introduction is relevant and significant.
Three maps and a glossary assist the reader.
