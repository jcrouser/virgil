Briggs' note shows how Vergil adapts part of the structure of a
passage
which is distinctively
epic in tone,
the loss of Eurydice
(G. 4.
481-527), and uses it twice in passages describing Aeneas' interviews with
Venus, and with Creusa, inAeneid 2. Deliberate variation of obvious repetition is characteristic of Vergil. Edgeworth notes that Vergil never mentions
the colour of honey but refers eight times to its liquid state. He understood
what chloros meant (liquid, moist) and sought to reflect that meaning inhis
choice of words. Harrison's retort to Richmond's discussion of Vergil's use of
symbols is concerned most with A. 1.31 4ff, Aeneas' encounter with Venus
disguised as Artemis-Diana.
Richter-Thill notes that Vergil accents relations
between
earthly creatures and
the world that envelops them. Schmiel examines
the
resonances of Catullus
101
(Multas per gentes et multa per
aequora vectus) in Vergil.
Usages occur in contexts of travel, death, and
human
loss. Shea notes analogies between Lucretius 6.172-203, and Aen.
2.52-6, and 8.407-53. Vergil borrows and interprets the Lucretian passage
within the framework of the Aeolus myth. Thomas suggests that white and
red flowers normally presage death (poppies, lilies, etc). Jointly, or individually, the two colours indicate present or imminent death.
