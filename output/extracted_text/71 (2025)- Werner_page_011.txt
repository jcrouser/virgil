Dardenay considers the question of the extent to which the Aeneid might
have influenced the iconographic representation of Rome’s founding
heroes. Gagliardi (2023a) notes that in his duels after the death of Pallas,
Aeneas often addresses his dying or dead adversaries with cruel words,
in contrast to their appeals to his pietas. Sometimes the dying enemies
respond to his sarcasm in a noble way that underscores their emotional
superiority. Gagliardi (2023b) notes that the verb immolare appears only
three times in Vergil’s entire work, all in the Aeneid and always referring
to the human sacrifices Aeneas makes in his anger at the death of Pallas
in 10.519 and 10.541 and in the murder of Turnus at the end of the poem
(12.949). Morgan brings out the further implications of these passages:
when Aeneas sacrifices the sons of Ufens at Aen. 10.515–20, Vergil deploys
a name expressive of Roman citizenship itself. Ufens evokes the tribus
Oufentina, established in 318 BCE as one of the thirty-five voting tribes
