Bernal offers a comprehensive philological study of the earliest printed
editions of the Aeneid in Spain (1500–1559). The emergence of the printing
press not only promoted the expansion of culture in general and classical
culture in particular, but also led to the birth of a new type of philology
and a new way of approaching Greek and Latin texts. Rodríguez-Noriega
Guillén takes as a starting point a poorly transmitted passage at Schol.
Veron. Aen. 3.705 for a discussion of the risks posed by the attribution
of fragments transmitted by sources with textual difficulties. She then
reviews the different proposals regarding the authorship of the passage
in question. Turcan writes that the illustrated Vergilius Vaticanus 3225
was produced by a Roman workshop around 400 CE but appears to derive
from an earlier archetype. Iconographic study suggests the existence of an
illustrated edition of Vergil on a volumen as early as the Severan period.
The incunabulum Paris, BNF, Rés. G. Yc. 236 contains notes by Poliziano
on the Eclogues and the Aeneid that contain comparisons with the Ajax,
the Oedipus Rex, and the Trachiniae by Sophocles, from which Vespoli
deduces that all the quotations by Poliziano coincide with the Sophoclean
text preserved in the tenth-century Florence, BML, MS plut. 32.09. Zurli
defends the reading dederis against dederit at Aen. 4.436.
