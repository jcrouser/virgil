Scholars continue to evidence fresh enthusiasm and imaginative insights in their study
of the pastoral poems. Bernard Dick's article concentrates on Vergil's use of the pathetic fallacy in the Eclogues, especially in Eclogue 10, and provides valuable comments
on this symbolic way of viewing reality. Kinzler (2) argues in favour of the future
perfect ('tulerint') but was challenged and routed by Huxley (3) and Baldwin (4) on
metrical grounds. Coleman (5) explores new aspects and new dimensions in Vergilian
pastoral in comparison with earlier writers. Eleanor Leach (6) finds careful balanced
construction in Eclogue 6, and Levi's lecture (7) is a masterly compression of useful
comments on Arcadia. Nethercut (8) attempts to corroborate Savage's earlier suggestion
(ТАРА, 94 (1963) 248-67) that Thyrsis in Eclogue 1 may be Horace. Van Sickle (9)
reformulates three familiar problems into one:
the unity of the Liber Bucolicorum,
the origin of Arcadian poetry, and the Vergilian imitation of Theocritus. Van Sickle's
paper deserves close attention and expansion. Skutsch's study of symmetry and sense
(10) is a masterly account of a basic principle. Steinmetz (11) revives the dramatic
aspects with skill and suasion. Wellesley (12) corroborates the text. Williams (13) and
Wormell (14) comment on style, allusions, and originality.
