templates an eventual return to his forfeited acres; the pessimism is sharp,
clear, and uncomprising. Rosemary Neilsen (2) finds political misgivings
in Е.1., a challenge to Octavian' s land policy, an appeal for the end
of fratricide and cruel expropriation. To win the divine status Octavian
must review his political programme. P.L. Smith (3) visualizes the avena
as an oatstraw monaulos, a primitive country instrument, which intensifies
the archetypal setting for the Eclogues and symbolizes the process of
pastoral composition. Yebra (4) deals with the firstlines of E. 1. Robertson
(5) studies E. 2 for adaptations, omissions and accretions of Theocritus
Idylls 3 and 11, and also finds affinities between Vergil's poetic lusus
and Gallus' poetic genre. Braun' s study (6) of the musical competition
in E. 3 and 7 marks no significant advances. Eleanor Leach (7) explores
the Platonic background ofthe Wunderkind in E. 4 and argues that allegorized myth is the spirit behind an idealistic, philosophical vision of human
government. Her awareness of the poem as a new image of the heroic
is likely to be influential in subsequent criticism. Stephen Waite's study
(8) of the contest in E. 7 is brief but enlightening. Bowersock's date
(10) has important implications for E. 8 and for the remainder of the
bucolic corpus. Carrière (12) studies V.'s lyrical manner in the bucolic
poems, a blend of description and contemplation in the manner of Keats,
Goethe, Lamartine etc. Brooks Otis (17) reviews his own interpretation
of the Eclogues in his monumental Virgil: A Study in Civilized Poetry
(Oxford, 1963). He finds that Klingner's literary sensitivity and sound
historical sense have brought readers perceptibly nearer to what Vergil
actually intended when he composed his bucolic poems. Otis' apologetic
review is required reading for students, and an incentive to closer scrutiny
of Klinger's Virgil (1967).
